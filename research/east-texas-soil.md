## Ranch Location — Pinned

## What the Soil Actually Is

**Sandy loam topsoil over red clay subsoil.** That's the official Panola County description from multiple sources including the Texas State Historical Association and Texas Almanac.

- **Surface layer**: Sandy loam — gray to light-colored, slightly acidic
- **Subsoil**: Sandy clay — reddish, good water retention, low in calcium
- **Classification**: Ultisol (highly weathered soils found across the southeastern US)
- **Piney Woods region**: East Texas soils belong to the "red and yellow soils group" characteristic of the southeastern United States

---

## Why It's Red (Iron Oxide, NOT Iron Ore)

The red color comes from **iron oxide** — rust, essentially. Weathered iron-bearing minerals in the parent rock break down over millennia through pressure, moisture, and biological activity. The iron oxidizes (rusts) and stains the clay particles red-orange.

**This is NOT iron ore.** Iron ore is a concentrated mineral deposit that can be mined for metal. Red clay is ordinary soil that contains dispersed iron oxide giving it color. You can't smelt a bucket of East Texas clay into anything useful — the iron content is too diffuse.

Think of it this way: iron ore is a vein of metal. Red clay is dirt that blushes.

## What the Ch1 Draft Currently Says

> "boots sunk two inches into the sandy clay that had built up around the base over twenty-two years of water splash and foot traffic, the red subsoil showing through where the topsoil had long since washed away"

**Verdict: Accurate.** Sandy clay at surface level around a wellhead (where splash erosion concentrates fines), red subsoil exposed where topsoil washed away. This is exactly what you'd see on a Panola County ranch around a 22-year-old wellhead.

## Additional Panola County Details (for prose accuracy)

- **County name**: Panola — from Choctaw word "ponolo" meaning cotton
- **Rainfall**: 43.9 inches/year average
- **Temperature range**: 34°F (Jan avg low) to 94°F (July avg high)
- **Trees**: Loblolly pine, shortleaf pine, post oak, hickory, elm, sweetgum, dogwood, magnolia
- **Wildlife**: Whitetail deer, squirrel, quail, wild turkey, feral hogs
- **Water**: Sabine River cuts through the county; Murvaul Lake, Martin Lake, Toledo Bend Reservoir nearby
- **Economy**: Natural gas (Haynesville Shale), timber, cattle, poultry (broilers)
- **Terrain**: Rolling hills, 172–548 ft elevation, pine forests, creek bottoms with bottomland hardwoods

## Why Carthage, Not Lufkin

**Carthage** is the county seat of Panola County — northeast East Texas, bordering Louisiana. Population ~7,000. Hardware stores, feed stores, Panola College. Twenty-six miles south of Marshall, sixty-five miles southeast of Tyler.

**Lufkin** is 100+ miles southwest in Angelina County — deep Piney Woods, bigger city (pop. ~35,000). Wrong part of East Texas for the ranch as currently described. Too far south, too urban.

Carthage is the right scale — small enough that Nate would know the hardware store owner by name, big enough to have what he needs for ranch supplies.
