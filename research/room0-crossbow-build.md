# Nate's Crossbow Build — Room 0 Scene Reference

*Reference for prose drafting. SA = Structural Analysis (Nate's glitched skill, Codex #87).*
*Created: Session 014, 2026-03-26*

---

## What Is Structural Analysis?

SA is Nate's first glitched skill. The System designed it to show players what IT wants them to see — item stats, quality ratings, System-approved data. Nate's version, warped by the occupied space during his partial 40% installation, shows him what is ACTUALLY THERE.

When SA fires, it's not a dramatic visual transformation. It's subtle — like putting on reading glasses and noticing fine print that was always there. The HUD flickers, the two faint green arcs pulse once, and whatever Nate is looking at becomes *legible.* He sees:

- **Material composition** — what something is made of at the structural level. Grain direction in wood. Crystalline structure in metal. Whether something is solid, hollow, composite, or generated filler.
- **Structural integrity** — where the stress points are, where something is load-bearing vs decorative, where it will fail under force. The weak weld. The thin spine. The crack that hasn't happened yet but will.
- **Procedural seams** — where the Tutorial's generation engine cut corners. Repeated patterns. Uniform density where natural variation should exist. The uncanny valley of manufactured materials pretending to be real ones.
- **What's real vs what's rendered** — the critical distinction. Some materials in the Tutorial are genuine — the System built them to be functional infrastructure (table legs, door brackets, torch mounts). Other materials are cosmetic — the System built them to LOOK like something without the substance behind it (weapon blades, armor plates, decorative finishes).

SA does NOT give Nate stat readouts. He doesn't see "Sword: +3 ATK, Durability 12/20." He sees the sword's spine is half the thickness it should be and the crossguard is cast alloy that'll crack under side-load. He reads materials the way a structural engineer reads a blueprint — or the way a shade-tree mechanic reads the underside of a truck. Not numbers. Understanding.

**In prose, SA is described experientially:**
- "The blade looked sharp. The steel told a different story — thin-gauge, hollow core, a decorative finish over nothing worth finishing."
- "The table leg rang when he tapped it. High and dense, not the dull thunk of composite filler. Actual wood. Straight grain. Built to hold weight."
- "His hands read the prod before his brain caught up — spring steel, properly tempered, the crystalline structure wanting to return to rest after deformation. This one piece was real."

## The Scene — Room 0, The Weapon Racks

### Setting

Fifty people in chaos. Screaming, crying, a man pounding on the wall looking for an exit. The children are gone — separated during Integration (Codex #83). Parents are frantic. Marcus Webb is already moving, organizing by instinct. "Anyone with military experience, over here."

Eight weapon racks line the walls. Maybe a hundred pieces of gear. Swords, shields, axes, staves, bows, crossbows, daggers, maces. Leather armor, robes, chain shirts. All of it has the too-clean look of things that were generated, not made.

Nate is standing in the middle of the room. Still shaking off Integration. Rex pressed against his left leg. HUD — two faint green arcs, barely visible, like parentheses made of light — flickering at the periphery. Flint running commentary Nate is only half hearing.

Then he looks at the weapon racks. SA activates.

---

### What SA Shows Him — The Weapons Are Garbage

Everyone else sees swords. Nate sees materials.

His vision shifts — not dramatically, not a special effect. Like focusing on a reflection in glass and suddenly seeing what was behind it all along. The green arcs pulse. The weapons become legible.

**The swords:** Pretty. Polished. Blades catch the torchlight and look heroic. SA reads them as thin-gauge steel with a decorative finish over a hollow core. The edge is real — sharp enough to cut — but the spine is half the thickness it should be. Three solid impacts against armor and the blade deforms. Five and it snaps. The crossguards are cast alloy, not forged — they'll crack at the weld under side-load. The handle wraps are uniform synthetic — no grain, no variation. Not real leather. A rendering of leather that has never been near an animal.

**Gamer lens:** "Starter gear. Gray-quality trash with good skins. Every game does this — gives you something that LOOKS functional for the first zone and breaks by the second."

**Engineer lens:** "Cosmetic steel over structural garbage. The System spent its render budget on appearance, not performance."

**The shields:** Same problem, different shape. Decorated faces — a lion on one, a tree on another — but the wood behind the face is generated composite. Not real wood. Two of the shields have bosses (the metal center piece) that SA reads as genuine forged iron — denser, heavier, actual crystalline structure instead of procedural fill. The bosses are real. Everything else is set dressing.

**The bows:** Recurve bows on one rack. SA shows limbs of generated composite — uniform grain, no natural variation, consistent density tip to tip. They'll fire arrows. But they have no life to them. Manufactured, not grown. The arrows are worse — straight enough but hollow composite shafts that will shatter on impact with anything harder than flesh.

People are grabbing the shiniest swords. The biggest shields. The most impressive-looking staves. Nobody is checking anything. Nobody is tapping the blade to hear the ring. Nobody is flexing the crossguard to test the weld.

Flint: "You're watching forty-eight people select weapons based on visual appeal while your Structural Analysis is cataloguing material deficiencies in every piece on the wall. I assume you have opinions."

"I have opinions."

"Would you like to share them or just stand here looking disappointed?"

---

### What SA Shows Him — The Crossbow

Three crossbows on the end of the second rack. Nobody's touching them — crossbows aren't sexy. In a room full of people grabbing swords, the crossbows are wallflowers.

SA reads them. Two are garbage — same cosmetic-over-hollow as the swords. Decorative prods that'll lose tension after twenty shots. Generated stocks that look like walnut but are the same uniform composite as everything else.

The third one is on the bottom of the rack. Partially hidden behind a shield nobody wanted. Ugly. The stock is warped softwood that won't hold zero. The trigger mechanism is sloppy cast metal with too much play in the lever. The rail is misaligned by two degrees — enough that every bolt would pull left.

But the prod — the bow part, the piece that stores and releases the energy — SA reads it and Nate stops.

**Spring steel. Actual temper.** The crystalline grain structure shows heat treatment — not uniform System generation but something that went through a real heating and cooling cycle. The metal wants to return to its resting state after deformation. It has memory. This one component was made right.

Nate's hands reach for it before his brain forms a reason. The rancher and the shade-tree mechanic don't think about good steel. They feel it. Twenty years of replacing trailer springs, checking hay baler teeth, testing the temper on farrier tools by the ring they make when you tap them. His hands know spring steel the way Rex knows cattle — at a level below language.

**Gamer lens:** "Hidden gem. The devs hid one good component in the trash pile. This is the 'inspect everything' reward that only the obsessive player finds."

**Engineer lens:** "The System built the prod to spec and cheaped out on everything else. Same thing happens at work — the contractor nails one critical component and cuts corners on the housing because the client only checks the output."

He pulls the crossbow off the rack. Strips the prod off the warped stock. Discards the body, the trigger, the rail. Keeps the one piece that matters and the bowstring — decent cordage, worth saving.

Somebody nearby — a Warrior type, big, already holding two swords he doesn't need — watches Nate tear apart a crossbow and keep one piece. "What are you doing?"

"Finding a good part."

"There's swords right there."

"I know."

The Warrior walks away with the expression of a man who has decided Nate is not his problem.

### The Build — At the Crafting Table

Josie Pickett is three feet to his left. He doesn't know her name yet. She's sorting through a pile of leather armor pieces and talking to them in a way that suggests she expects them to answer.

**The stock:** Nate looks at the crafting table. SA reads the table itself — structural, built to hold weight. The System generated it for function, not appearance. The table legs are straight-grain hardwood, dense, load-bearing. One leg is slightly thicker than the others — overbuilt for its role. More material than necessary. That leg is better wood than anything in the weapon racks.

"You're looking at the table like the table owes you money," Flint says.

"The table leg is better wood than every weapon handle in the room."

"I'm going to regret saying this, but: explain."

"The System built the weapons to look good. It built the table to hold weight. The table doesn't need to impress anyone. It just needs to work. So the System used real material." He taps the leg. High, dense ring — not a dull thunk. "Hear that? That's actual hardwood. Straight grain. Twenty inches of stock that'll hold a prod better than anything on those racks."

"You're going to take the table apart."

"I'm going to take one leg."

"The table has four legs."

"It'll manage."

He uses the forging hammer from the crafting station — flat face and cross peen, solid steel, the kind of tool that was built for hitting metal, not nails. He works the joint until the leg releases. Twenty inches of straight hardwood. Feels right in his hands. Feels like a stock.

**The trigger:** A metal bracket on the wall holds a torch in place. SA reads it — forged iron, simple, dense, a U-shaped bracket with bolt holes. He pries it off the wall with the hammer. Files the bolt holes wider using the rough edge of the crafting table's iron banding. Bends the bracket into a trigger lever using the table edge as a brake.

It's ugly. It'll work.

**The assembly:** Prod mounted to the hardwood stock. Field Repair activates when his hands touch both pieces simultaneously — the molecular-level connection he's been feeling since his skills glitched online. Not welding. Not gluing. Convincing the materials to hold each other. The wood grain tightens around the prod's mounting surface. The connection is stronger than anything the System's assembly would have produced because Field Repair doesn't follow manufacturing specifications. It follows material preference. It asks the wood how it wants to hold the steel, and the wood answers.

Trigger mechanism set into a notch he carved with the hammer's peen. Bowstring from the original crossbow — threaded, tensioned, tested.

Grip wrapped in a strip of leather. Not the synthetic stuff from the armor rack — the real strip from the inside of a belt buckle, where the Tutorial generated actual leather to hold the buckle in place and nobody would ever think to check.

**Ten minutes.** The crossbow looks like someone built it in a barn. The stock is a table leg. The trigger is a wall bracket. The prod is the only honest piece of steel in the room.

It is ugly in the way that honest things are ugly and dangerous in the way that engineered things are dangerous.

Flint: "I have catalogued what you just did and I want you to know that from a strictly technical standpoint, you built a ranged weapon from furniture and wall fixtures while forty-eight people selected purpose-built armaments designed for combat."

"Theirs will break."

"Mine won't?"

"Theirs will break first."

### Josie — First Contact

She's been watching. Not the whole time — she was busy sorting her own pile — but she caught the moment he pulled the table leg free and she hasn't looked away since.

"You just built a weapon out of the room."

Nate doesn't look up. He's testing the trigger pull. Click. Reset. Click. Smooth enough. "I built a crossbow out of parts."

"Out of the ROOM. Out of a table and a wall bracket and a crossbow nobody wanted." She's looking at the weapon racks where fifty people are grabbing swords. She's looking at Nate's hands. She's making a connection that nobody else in the room is making. "You can see what things actually are."

"I can see what things are made of."

"That's the same thing."

It isn't. But it's close enough that he looks at her for the first time. Short. Compact. Dark ponytail. Brown eyes that move like they're scanning inventory, which — he'll learn later — they are.

"You grabbed the only real leather in the room," she says. "The belt backing. Nobody else would have found that."

"How do you know it's real?"

She holds up a strip of the synthetic leather from the armor pile in one hand and the real strip she'd already pulled from a different belt in the other. "Because this one has grain and this one doesn't. I run estate sales. I know the difference between real and fake leather before I touch it."

Flint, internally: "I like her."

Nate, internally: *Not now.*

Flint: "No, I'm serious. Her material identification is operating at a frequency I find professionally interesting."

### The Hammer — The Afterthought That Matters More

The crossbow was the statement. The hammer is the afterthought.

He picks the forging hammer back up — the one from the crafting station. Flat face on one side, cross peen on the other. It was always the backup. He grabbed it first because it felt right, the way reaching for a tool always feels right when the world stops making sense.

SA reads it: solid head, decent steel, handle could be better. Forging hammer — made for shaping metal, not pulling nails. The flat face is for general striking. The cross peen is for drawing out material, starting grooves, working edges. This is a blacksmith's tool. It ended up on a crafting table in a Tutorial pocket because the System needed something to fill the "hammer" slot in the starter crafting kit and accidentally put a real one there.

He wraps the handle with cordage for grip. Tightens the head connection with Field Repair — the steel seats deeper into the wooden handle, the fit going from adequate to permanent. Adds a loop so he can hang it from his belt.

Two minutes. Nobody notices. Everyone's watching the Warriors compare swords.

The hammer will be in his hand at every crafting station between combat rooms. It will forge thorn bolts, repair armor, improve gear, reinforce the crossbow stock. It will occasionally hit a Thornling that gets too close. And in Book 2, when Nate discovers his forge, this hammer — improved, upgraded, reinforced a hundred times over but still recognizably the same tool — will strike the first blow on the first piece of pre-System metal he ever works.

The crossbow is his weapon. The hammer is his purpose.
