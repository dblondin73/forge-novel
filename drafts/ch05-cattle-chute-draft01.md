# Chapter 5 — Cattle Chute

---

They came out of the ground like something that had been planted wrong.

The first one surfaced three feet from a woman who'd been a Mage for less than an hour. She screamed — not a combat sound, not a tactical response, just the raw human noise of watching the earth split open and something with bark for skin and thorns for fingers pull itself free. The creature was fast, goblin-sized, hunched on all fours with arms too long for its body and eyes that pulsed the same dim rhythm Nate had been watching through the spawn points. It smelled like sap and turned earth and something underneath both that was older and less honest.

The Mage threw fire. Blue-white, System-generated, clean and perfect and completely panicked — the fireball cracked the creature's bark-skin along its shoulder and sent it stumbling sideways, but it didn't go down. It turned toward the fire with the blind focus of something that had been built to take hits and keep coming.

Twelve more pushed through in the next three seconds.

Nate grabbed the post driver from where it leaned against the rock face and got his back square against the stone. The clearing had become a geometry problem — two hundred people, fifteen spawn points, four corridor exits, and a dozen creatures that moved in coordinated arcs like they'd been rehearsing this for weeks. Which, Nate supposed, they had. The spawn points were the rehearsal. The creatures were the performance.

"Pack hunters," Flint said, his voice running fast but controlled — the field-report cadence, not the show. "Six moving left toward the Mage cluster. Three circling right toward the Warriors. Three coming straight at you."

Nate didn't wait for them to arrive.

Ranch rules. First rule: control the space. A fence line is forty inches of taut wire and the ten feet on either side of it where cattle can't quite figure out what they're supposed to do. Nate's space was the rock face behind him and the eight-foot arc the post driver could reach. Anything inside that arc was going to get sixteen pounds of improved steel in the teeth.

The first creature came upright and lunged — thorn-studded hands reaching, bark-skin rasping against the air, making a sound that was closer to a branch snapping than anything alive should make. Nate swung from the hips, the way you swing a post driver when you're setting T-posts in hardpan — not at the creature but through it, the weight of the tool doing the work, the arc carrying all the way to the backstop.

The cap connected with its ribcage. The bark-skin cracked like firewood. The thing folded around the impact point and went sideways, trailing amber sap that smelled like pine resin mixed with something that wanted to be blood but didn't quite know how.

Second rule: don't get surrounded.

The other two came together — flanking, coordinated, one high and one low. Nate reset his grip and —

Rex.

The dog was a black-and-white streak along the ground, thirty-five pounds of Border Collie moving the way Border Collies move when the herd goes wrong — not charging, not attacking, but *positioning*. Rex dropped into the working crouch six feet to Nate's left and locked eyes with the flanking creature.

The stare. The one that had held a breakaway heifer at a gate for twenty minutes. The one that said, in a language older than words or System interfaces or whatever the hell was happening to the world: *You will not go that way. You will go where I tell you.*

The creature hesitated. Just a half-second — a hitch in its coordinated movement, a moment of uncertainty from something that had never been looked at by an animal that understood pressure and position at a level its pack programming couldn't parse. The hesitation bent its path, pushed it three degrees to the right, and Nate was already swinging.

The post driver caught it under the jaw. Bark-skin disintegrated. Amber sap sprayed across the rock face.

Rex didn't look at the kill. He was already repositioning, reading the next pressure point in the clearing the way he read cattle flow — which creature was about to break, which direction the pack was leaning, where the pressure needed to go next.

Across the clearing, Marcus Webb was doing something similar with humans.

"Warriors, hold the east corridor entrance! Mages, rear support — don't crowd the line, give them room to swing! Anyone without a combat class, move to the rock face, stay behind the fighters, and for God's sake stop standing on the spawn points!"

His voice carried the way it always had in project meetings — not loud, but shaped to fill whatever space needed filling. Tactical Coordinator wasn't just a class the System had assigned him. It was a diagnosis.

The fighting lasted four minutes. Four minutes of bark-skin cracking and amber sap and the sounds that came out of people discovering that their bodies could do things their minds hadn't caught up to yet — a Warrior whose sword technique connected with a precision he didn't own, a Mage whose second fireball hit with twice the force of the first because the System was feeding her combat data in real time through a screen she was still learning to read.

Nate fought like a man setting fence in a high wind. No techniques. No System-fed precision. Just sixteen pounds of steel and the geometry of keeping his back to something solid and everything else at arm's length. He dropped three more creatures in the arc in front of the rock face. Rex herded two into the Warriors' kill zone near the east corridor. One Healer went down with bark thorns across her forearm before a Shield Guardian — big man, construction worker's hands — stepped in front of her and took the next hit on a shield that existed because the System said it did.

Then it was over.

---

The bodies dissolved. That was the part that made Nate's stomach turn — not the fighting, which had its own logic, but the aftermath. The creatures collapsed into amber sap and sawdust, the material seeping back into the generated ground, the Tutorial reclaiming its construction materials for the next wave. Recycling. The engineer in him saw the efficiency. The rancher in him recognized composting when he saw it.

"Twelve down," Flint said. "Fourteen wounded on our side — nothing fatal, the Healers are handling it. Two spawn points depleted. Thirteen still active." A pause. "They're recharging, Nate. The interval is —" Flint went quiet for two seconds, which was a long time for Flint. "Four minutes. Maybe five. The rhythm's not steady — it's adaptive. The more quickly we killed them, the shorter the interval."

"The Tutorial's adjusting to our performance."

"I would like to find that surprising. I don't."

Nate sat down with his back against the rock face. Rex materialized at his left knee — not panting, not shaking, just present, eyes still tracking the clearing with the flat working stare that hadn't changed since they'd arrived. Something about the dog felt different, though. Not wrong — *more*. Like a radio station that had been slightly off-frequency his whole life and had suddenly locked in. The impressions coming through their bond were sharper now, clearer — not words, never words, but the difference between hearing music through a wall and hearing it through an open window.

*Clear. For now. Not for long.*

The impression was so precise that Nate looked down at Rex. The dog met his eyes, held them for one second, then went back to scanning. No explanation offered. None needed.

Nate set the post driver across his knees and wrapped both hands around the steel handle.

Field Repair activated on contact — the same shift he'd felt in the clearing before the wave, the same language of stress and grain and molecular structure flowing through his palms. But now it came easier, faster, as if combat had loosened something in the connection between his hands and the skill. He could feel the weld near the cap — the metal fatigue he'd known about for months but could now read the way a surgeon reads an X-ray. The weld was weak on the north side, where heat distribution had been uneven during manufacturing. Three more solid hits and it would crack. Ten more and the cap would separate from the tube entirely.

His hands knew what the steel needed before his brain formed the question.

He pressed his thumb into the weak point and felt something shift under the pressure — not physically, not with muscle, but through the skill. The molecular structure tightened. The crystalline grain realigned along the stress axis. The weld didn't disappear; it hardened, densified, became the strongest point on the tool instead of the weakest. The steel under his palms went from *fence tool that's seen better days* to *fence tool that will see considerably more days than anything that gets in its way.*

"You didn't make a weapon," Flint said quietly. "You fixed the one you already had."

Nate stood up and looked at the clearing with Structural Analysis.

The spawn points were recharging — pulse rate climbing, buried rhythm accelerating toward the frequency that meant *opening soon*. But he wasn't watching the spawn points. He was watching the corridors.

Four passages radiating out from the clearing like spokes. Groups had drifted toward the entrances during the fight — combat momentum carrying people outward, away from the central clearing, into the Tutorial's architecture. A cluster of Warriors and two Mages had pushed twenty yards into the east corridor, chasing the last creatures. A mixed group had drifted south.

The corridors weren't random. Structural Analysis showed him the pattern — the same one he'd seen when they first arrived, but now, after watching how the creatures moved and where the spawn points pushed survivors, the shape of it was unmistakable. Narrowing at forty yards: chokepoint. Widening at eighty: rest node. Spawn points clustered at transitions between zones, placed to push groups forward, toward encounters, toward the next node in the chain.

"It's herding them." Flint said it before Nate could form the words — the AI reading the same pattern through Nate's visual data and getting to the conclusion half a second faster. "Same way you move cattle through a chute. Crowd them, pressure them from behind, narrow the path so they can only go forward. The Tutorial isn't testing people. It's *processing* them."

Nate was quiet for three seconds.

"That was my line."

"You were too slow. I have to work with what I've got." But underneath the speed, through their shared space, Nate felt something that wasn't humor. Worry. The Tutorial had been designed to sort two hundred people into manageable streams and feed them through escalating encounters. The people who'd drifted into the corridors were already being processed. And the algorithm was adaptive.

"We need to stay in the clearing," Nate said. "Anyone who goes into those corridors is getting funneled."

"And the spawn points in the clearing?"

"We can handle the spawn points. We can see them." He looked at the corridors — at the people already twenty yards deep, at the narrowing walls and the spawn points he could see clustered ahead of them like mines in a road. "They can't."

He started walking toward the east corridor entrance where the Warriors had pushed in. Not running. Walking, because panic was contagious and calm was too, and right now the clearing needed the second one more than the first.

"Hey." Loud enough to carry, quiet enough not to sound like an order. "Pull back to the clearing. There's more of those things seeded down the corridor — twelve spawn points in the first sixty yards."

The lead Warrior — a man in mechanic's coveralls with a System-granted short sword that looked absurd in his oil-stained hands — turned and looked at Nate. Looked at the PROCESSING tag above his head. Looked at the corridor ahead, which was dark and narrowing and felt wrong in the way that only a structure designed to process you could feel wrong.

"How do you know that?"

"Because I can see them."

The mechanic didn't ask for credentials. He came back.

---

Rex's ears snapped forward four seconds before the spawn points lit up.

The dog went rigid — not the working crouch but something more intense, head turned thirty degrees north, body aimed like a compass needle at a point in the ground that Structural Analysis hadn't flagged yet. Through the empathic link, Nate felt it — not a thought, not a word, but a directional pressure so precise it might as well have been a finger pointing: *THERE. NOW.*

He looked. A spawn point he'd mapped as dormant was cycling. Fast.

"North cluster, three points, activating out of sequence," Flint confirmed, his voice half a beat behind what Rex had already delivered. "They're not following Wave 1's pattern. The algorithm adjusted."

"How many?"

"More."

Fifteen creatures pushed through in the first five seconds — half again as many as Wave 1, coming from three directions instead of one. And behind the smaller ones, something else entirely.

It stood six feet tall where the others barely reached four. The bark-skin on its chest and shoulders had densified into plates — layered, overlapping, the same composite material but compressed into something that looked like armor and, through Structural Analysis, functioned like it. Thorn protrusions along its forearms were longer, thicker, angled forward like tools designed for a specific purpose. Its eyes didn't pulse. They burned — a steady amber glow that was the spawn-point rhythm locked to permanent.

"That's new," Flint said. "Bigger. Armored. I'm calling it a Thornguard because no one else is naming things and someone should."

Three Warriors converged on the Thornguard instinctively — combat classes reading a threat hierarchy that their System-granted instincts flagged as *priority target*. A sword technique carved a line across the bark plating. The plate held. A second Warrior drove his weapon into the shoulder joint — better placement, the gap between plates — and amber sap welled out, but the Thornguard turned and backhanded him with a thorn-studded arm that sent him skidding ten feet across the clearing on his back.

"The plating is non-uniform," Nate said, reading the creature through SA the way he'd read the Tutorial architecture — looking for the seams, the stress points, the places where the procedural generation had cut corners. "Chest plates overlap, but the lower back is single-layer. The arm joints are exposed between extension and reset. And the forearm thorns are structural — break one and the arm loses a third of its striking power."

"You're doing tactical readouts now."

"I'm reading what I see."

He raised his voice — not Marcus's carry-to-the-back projection, but enough to reach the Warriors within earshot. "The big one — hit it from behind, lower back. The armor's thin. And the arm thorns are load-bearing — snap them and the arms go weak."

The Shield Guardian — the same man who'd dismissed Nate's PROCESSING tag an hour ago — looked at him. Looked at the Thornguard. Made a tactical decision that had nothing to do with class designations and everything to do with being chest-deep in something that was winning. He circled.

The Thornguard went down thirty seconds later. Lower back, single-layer plate, two coordinated strikes from Warriors who'd never fought together before but could follow clear instructions when the instructions came from someone who could see what they couldn't.

The regular creatures had used the Thornguard as cover — swarming the flanks while the big target drew Warrior focus. Classic pack behavior. Nate recognized it because Rex recognized it, and Rex's recognition flowed through the bond like a diagram overlaid on the battle: *here's the flow, here's the pressure, here's where it breaks if you stand HERE.*

Nate stood there. Two creatures came through his arc. The post driver — improved, heavy, swinging with the full certainty of a man whose hands knew what the steel could do — ended both conversations.

Between swings, his free hand found a fragment of rock at the base of the wall. Field Repair read it on contact — the crystalline composite, dense, with a natural fracture plane that wanted to become an edge. He didn't have time to work it now. He shoved it into his back pocket and filed the knowledge: the Tutorial was full of parts. He just needed time to assemble them.

Rex worked the perimeter the way he'd worked cattle his whole life — never engaging directly but always *present*, always applying pressure that bent the pack coordination into shapes the creatures didn't intend. One that should have flanked the Mage cluster veered right instead, driven by thirty-five pounds of Border Collie stare, and ran straight into a Warrior's waiting blade.

The dog was herding monsters. And the monsters had no idea what herding was.

"That's not something he learned," Flint said, very quietly. "That's something he always knew how to do. It's just... more, now."

"Yeah," Nate said, swinging the post driver through a creature that had gotten too close to a frozen Healer. "I noticed."

The second wave broke. More amber sap, more sawdust, more of the Tutorial digesting its own dead. The Thornguard's remains took longer to dissolve — the bark plates resisting the reclamation process the way a hard drive resists reformatting, giving up its data reluctantly and in chunks.

Nate counted the spawn points. Five depleted now. Ten active. Recharge interval shorter than before.

More people had drifted into the corridors during the fighting, pulled by momentum and panic and the natural human impulse to run *away* from the thing trying to kill you, even when *away* led straight into a chute.

"Three minutes," Flint said. "Maybe less. And Nate — the corridor spawn points are accelerating independent of the clearing. The Tutorial is running parallel processing. Different streams, different encounter schedules."

"Can you track the people who went in?"

"Fragments. Network access is —" Flint paused. "I can see movement. I can't see outcomes."

Nate pulled the rock fragment from his pocket. He had maybe two minutes. He sat against the wall and held the composite in both hands, letting Field Repair map its internal structure — the crystalline grain, the fault lines, the planes where the material wanted to separate if asked correctly. He found the fracture point. Struck the fragment against the rock face behind him — one clean crack, precise, along the natural plane.

The composite separated into a wedge. Not pretty. Not symmetrical. But the edge was real — dense enough to gouge bark plates, sharp enough to find the gaps between them. A tool made from the Tutorial's own materials, built by a man who could read what things wanted to become.

He was still holding it when the ground started to pulse again.

---

The Tutorial stopped being polite.

Spawn points fired from three corridors simultaneously — not the clearing this time but the threshold zones where the spokes met the hub. Creatures poured through like water through breached levees, twenty or more in the first surge, and behind them two Thornguards moving with the deliberate patience of things that knew the smaller ones would absorb the first response.

Marcus's line buckled.

Not all at once — not a collapse but an erosion. The east corridor entrance, where the Warriors had been holding formation, took the heaviest wave. A Thornguard hit the shield line at full stride and the Shield Guardian who'd killed the first one discovered that the second one had been paying attention. It feinted the lower back, drew the circling response, then pivoted and drove a thorn-studded arm through the gap the pivot created. The Shield Guardian went down with bark thorns through his shield arm, and the line behind him — Mages who'd been providing ranged support, a Healer who'd been positioned too close — scattered.

"East line is breaking," Flint said. Fast, stripped-to-the-studs fast, no decoration. "South corridor taking incursions. West is holding but they're being compressed inward. The clearing is becoming a kill box if we don't consolidate."

"Where are the active spawns?"

"Eight. All cycling. Nate, the recharge interval just dropped to ninety seconds."

The woman who'd been calling her child's name since they arrived was still on her knees near the south corridor. Creatures flowed around her like water around a stone — not mercy, just algorithmic triage. She wasn't registering as a combatant. The System didn't waste resources on non-threats.

Nate felt Rex before he saw him.

Through the bond — sharper now than it had been even ten minutes ago, the open-window clarity cranked to something that bordered on seeing through the dog's eyes — a map of the entire clearing rendered not in images but in pressure and motion. Rex wasn't tracking individual creatures anymore. He was reading the *wave* — the whole incoming mass as a single entity with momentum and pressure points and places where it would break if you applied force in the right direction. The way he'd read sixty-three head of Red Angus his whole working life, except the herd was made of thorns and the gate was a rock face and the stakes were considerably higher than a loose heifer.

*Pull back. Rock face. Now.*

The impression hit like a shout Nate could feel in his chest.

"Rex is right," Flint said, and it was the first time he'd ever cited the dog's input as tactical data worth acting on. "The rock face is the only defensible position. Everything else is exposed to multi-directional spawn."

Nate didn't shout. He didn't need to. Marcus did it for him.

The project manager had already read the same collapse — through different eyes but toward the same conclusion. Marcus Webb had spent twenty years managing complex systems with too many variables and not enough resources, and a Tutorial combat zone wasn't structurally different from a government program going sideways. You identified the stable node, you routed everything toward it, and you made damn sure the stable node held.

"Fall back to the rock face! Everyone — rock face, now! Fighters, hold while others move, then pull back in pairs!"

People moved. Not smoothly, not cleanly — a retreat under pressure never is — but they moved, because Marcus's voice carried the quality that command voices have when they're backed by competence instead of rank. The quality that makes people believe the person talking might actually know where safety is.

Nate anchored the rock face. Post driver in his right hand, the rock wedge in his left — dense composite, sharp enough to gouge, solid enough to survive impact. He fought with both hands. Post driver for the sweeping work — heavy, formation-breaking, the tool that cracked bark plating and sent creatures spinning. Rock wedge for the close work — the tool that found the gaps between plates and drove deep, the tool his Field Repair had built from the Tutorial's own infrastructure.

Two tools. Neither of them weapons by design. Both of them weapons by application.

Rex worked the retreat perimeter — not just herding creatures now but herding *people*, positioning himself between the stragglers and the incoming wave, using body pressure and that unbreakable Border Collie stare to push panicking humans toward the rock face. A Healer who'd frozen in the open felt something press against her calf — warm, solid, insistent — and looked down to find a black-and-white dog staring up at her with an expression that said, in a language that didn't require a System interface to translate: *Move. That direction. Now.*

She moved.

The group compressed. Forty people against the rock face — fighters on the outside, Mages behind them, Healers working the wounded in the deepest recess. The rest of the pocket — the ones who'd gone into the corridors, the ones who'd drifted too far, the ones who hadn't heard Marcus or hadn't listened — were somewhere in the chute now. Being processed. Nate hoped they'd find rest nodes. He couldn't help them from here.

What he could do was hold this position.

The third wave hit the rock face line like a river hitting a dam — hard, sustained, relentless. Three corridors' worth of creatures converging on the one position in the clearing that refused to scatter. A Thornguard came straight at the center, bark plates gleaming with the amber sap of smaller creatures it had walked through to get here. Warriors met it — three of them, coordinated now, working the lower-back weakness Nate had called out during Wave 2 without being told twice. The Thornguard lasted fifteen seconds. The Warriors were learning.

The smaller creatures kept coming. The spawn points kept cycling. Nate stopped counting kills and started counting intervals — how long between waves, how many spawn points still active, how much the algorithm was adjusting. The numbers were moving in the wrong direction. Faster cycles, heavier concentrations, the Tutorial pouring resources into the clearing because the clearing wasn't supposed to still be occupied. The algorithm expected scattered groups in corridors. It got a consolidated force at a defensible position.

"Spawn points in the clearing are cycling down," Flint said, and there was something in his voice that might have been cautious optimism wearing its most skeptical outfit. "Five depleted, three more approaching threshold. The corridor spawns are accelerating, but they're targeting the groups in the chute, not us. The Tutorial is redirecting resources toward the people it can herd. It's —" Flint stopped. "Nate, it's giving up on you."

"What?"

"Not giving up. Deprioritizing. You broke the algorithm. You were supposed to scatter into the corridors like everyone else. Instead you consolidated, held position, and refused to be processed. The Tutorial's efficiency model is routing around you the way traffic routes around a roadblock."

The last creature in the third wave went down under a combined strike from two Warriors — the mechanic with the short sword and a woman in hospital scrubs whose class designation said Battle Medic and whose combat technique suggested the System had given her something worth keeping. They'd figured out, in the last twenty minutes, that fighting near the man with the fence tool led to better outcomes than following their System-assigned instincts into dark corridors.

The clearing went quiet. The spawn points dimmed — not depleted, not dead, but cycling down to a low dormant pulse that Nate's SA read as *interval reset: extended*. The Tutorial was catching its breath. So were they.

Rex circled the group once — a full perimeter check, nose working, ears rotating, every sense dialed to a frequency that a normal Border Collie shouldn't have been able to reach — then came back to Nate's left knee and sat. Not collapsed. Sat. Alert, ready, unbroken. A working dog who had found new work and was waiting for the next task with the patient expectation of an animal who had never in his life considered the possibility that his person might not have a plan.

Nate looked at the group. Forty people. Some wounded, all scared, none of them quit. And they were looking at him — not at Marcus, not at the Warriors with their System-granted sword techniques, not at the Mages with their combat classes and spell lists. At the man with no class, no designation, and a fence tool that had no business being in a combat zone but had refused to break.

He hadn't asked for it. He hadn't wanted it. But the thing about people following you isn't that you choose it — it's that they do, and once they've chosen, the only honest response is to be worth following.

"The corridors are a trap," he said. His voice was unhurried, low, the register that didn't need volume to fill a silence. "The Tutorial is designed to herd us through them. We stay in the clearing, we fight on our terms. We control the chute instead of being pushed through it."

Marcus stepped up beside him. Not behind, not in front. Beside. The Tactical Coordinator and the man who could see the architecture, shoulder to shoulder, each one doing the thing the other couldn't.

"You heard him," Marcus said. "We hold here. We fight smart. And the next wave that comes through that ground is going to find out what happens when the cattle turn around and face the damn chute."

Nobody cheered. Nobody clapped. Forty people — bruised, bleeding, holding abilities they'd had for less than two hours and weapons they didn't fully understand — just nodded. The quiet nod of people who'd been given a plan by someone who could see what they couldn't, and had decided that seeing was enough.

The corridors stretched ahead of them — longer, darker, and seeded with things that made the first three waves look like the rough draft of something worse.

But they'd held. And for now, for this moment, in a Tutorial pocket that the System had built to process them and they had chosen to defy, that was enough.

Rex pressed his shoulder against Nate's shin. The weight of the dog was warm and certain and exactly the same as it had been on the ranch that morning, before the countdown, before the white, before the world became something none of them had asked for.

Some things the System couldn't touch. Some things were older than the overlay and would outlast it.

The post driver was warm in Nate's hands. The rock wedge sat heavy in his back pocket. And the spawn points beneath his feet pulsed their slow dormant rhythm, counting down to something that would come whether he was ready or not.

He'd be ready.
