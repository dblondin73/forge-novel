# Chapter 5 — First Blood

---

The Tutorial had lessons, mind. Nobody had asked the fifty people walking through the dark whether they'd signed up.

---

## I. Corridor Approach

The passage funneled them forward — fifty people moving through a dim stone corridor with nothing but the sound of their own breathing and something scratching beneath the floor ahead.

Then the walls fell away and they were in a chamber, and the scratching stopped.

Nate liked the scratching better.

**Structural Analysis** had been tracking displacement patterns under the stone for the last forty seconds. Six points where the floor's load distribution shifted in slow sequence — something pressing upward, testing the stone from below. Load-bearing material bending under internal force, the substrate deforming in concentric stress patterns around each point. Something was coming. The only question was *when.*

The blue arcs at the edges of his vision — the two parenthesis curves he'd carried since the HUD booted — brightened. Then a chip of light resolved just outside the left arc, high up: front-left. A second bloomed past the arc's midpoint: directly left. A third below that. The chips lit in sequence around him, tracing the same slow rhythm as the pressure points under the stone. Not targeting. Not information he could read. Just peripheral glows that said *there, and there, and there.* The HUD responding to adrenaline, or to threat, or to whatever instinct lived in the gap between conscious thought and the thing that made his hands steady on a rifle when the coyotes came out at dusk.

The chamber was forty feet across. Stone walls, arched ceiling, dim torchlight that made everything the color of rust. Flint tagged the compass in Nate's peripheral — entrance behind them as north, spawn points ahead as south. Standard callout grid.

Rex set at Nate's left knee. Judge set at his right — stub tail vibrating at a frequency that suggested opinions were already forming about everything in the room. Both dogs watching the south wall with the unblinking focus Nate had seen a thousand times on the ranch. The kind of focus that arrived when the cattle knew something was out there before the humans did.

Marcus Webb moved into the front of the group, Vanguard class and twenty years of leadership putting him exactly where he was supposed to be. His voice came low and even. "Warriors forward. Mages second rank. Supports and unarmed behind the warrior line — hug the north wall. Nobody panics."

Fifty. That was the number Nate was walking in with. He'd counted in the Safe Room — old habit, his father's teaching.

Spawn point three cracked first. Bark-covered fingers pushed through the stone. Then the whole thing erupted upward in a shower of dirt and rock, and the room went from silence to pandemonium in about two seconds.

---

## II. First Wave

The other five spawn points opened in a staggered rip — gravel and stone-dust hazing the air, the smell sharp and mineral, like a freshly broken concrete floor. Someone screamed — the high animal sound of a person whose nervous system had outpaced their thinking by half a second. Another screamed back. A man in the support line dropped his unfamiliar weapon and reached for it twice on the way to the floor before he picked it up.
"Thornling," Flint said. Quick, clipped, reading from somewhere Nate couldn't see. "System-generated combatant. Bark-plated, thorn-studded, approximately three and a half feet tall. Think 'angry topiary' and you're most of the way there."

Six of them. Pulling themselves out of the stone with the jerky, root-finding motion of saplings in a time-lapse, if saplings had bark-plate armor and thorn-studded arms and the blind hunger of things grown to kill.

"HOLD!" Marcus bellowed. "Warriors engage! Mages second rank! HOLD!"

The warriors were already charging before the words landed. The System had spent the last hour downloading one basic technique per weapon into their muscles — one swing, one stance, one way to hold the blade — and even that single skill wanted to be used. New truck, open road, foot itching for the gas.

A big man with a longsword — Kyle Greene, if Nate's name-match from the corridor held — swung at the nearest Thornling with the jerky confidence of a man whose muscles had received instructions his body hadn't fully learned yet. The blade found the general area between shoulder and torso — not clean, not precise, but hard enough to bite into the bark-skin. The Thornling screamed — the cracking sound of branches in an ice storm, high and wrong and not quite alive enough to feel sorry for.

Amber sap wept from the wound. Thick, viscous, the color of pine resin on a hot day. **Structural Analysis** read the site automatically — bark-plate compromised, fibrous interior exposed, stress fracture radiating from the point of impact. The longsword had done its job. Not pretty, but the post was in the ground.

Two more warriors closed on the Thornling beside it. A stocky woman — Mack Turner, Nate thought, though he was guessing — swung a mace in a textbook arc for the half-second the System steered it. Wide arc, rotation through the hip, weight transfer perfect. Then her body lost the thread, the follow-through collapsed, and the mace head clipped the Thornling instead of crushing it. A teenager with a shield-and-shortsword combo — Pete Bowman, Nate was pretty sure — did better. The shortsword was small enough that the downloaded technique didn't fight him as hard, and his thrust landed in the bark-plate flush. Not deep. But seated. He yanked it free with both hands and didn't fall over, which, by Rank 1 Low standards, was currently a victory.

"Every combat skill in this room is Rank 1 Low," Flint said internally. Tactical register — fast, no jokes, pure data. "Novice tier. The System downloaded aim, timing, technique — the knowledge is there, but their bodies are still arguing with the instructions. Think of it as a GPS in a car that doesn't have power steering."

Which explained the **Mage**.

She was maybe twenty-five — Heather Kim, per the Safe Room roll — and the **Fire Bolt** she threw from the second rank hit a Thornling in the chest hard enough to ignite bark in a flare of orange light that made the whole room flinch. The System had given her the knowledge — how to gather mana, how to shape it, where to aim. But knowledge wasn't control. She'd panicked and dumped everything she had behind it — too much mana in a single cast, the magical equivalent of flooring a truck in first gear. **Structural Analysis** read the aftermath: surface burns, structural damage minimal. The Thornling staggered but didn't drop. The **Mage**'s hands were already dimmer. Somewhere a resource management tutorial was weeping.

Behind her, a second-rank buffer stammered his way through a word Nate couldn't hear. A skinny man with a focus-stone clutched like it was going to bite him. A soft pulse of pale light washed over the nearest Warrior's shield. The shield's rim glowed for maybe four seconds, then faded. The Warrior didn't notice. The buffer noticed. His hands were shaking and the focus-stone was bright and his eyes were wider than Heather's.

*Support class,* Flint said. *Minor hardening. If I'm reading the residual signature correctly — and I stress if the spec sheet I can read is any guide — he just added about fifteen percent durability to that shield for twenty seconds. He doesn't know he did it. The shield doesn't know it got it. The Thornling on the other side of that shield will, in approximately six seconds, not know it either.*

To Kyle's left, a **Healer** named Ana Torres was already moving. A Warrior — a thick-built woman Nate had pegged in the Safe Room as Isabel Wu — had taken a thorn across the forearm on her second swing, and the cut had opened to the bone. Ana got there before the Warrior finished shouting. One palm flat to the wound, the other cupped under Isabel's elbow, and a white light the color of clean winter sun bloomed out of her fingers and into the flesh. The cut stitched. Muscle rewove under the skin in motion that should have taken hours and took four seconds. Ana watched it happen with her mouth half-open, eyes wider than Isabel's — the face of a woman who'd just discovered her own hands did a thing her brain was still trying to catch up to.

Isabel looked down, looked at Ana, didn't bother saying anything, turned back to the Thornling and drove her blade into its throat.

"**Healer** first cast," Flint said. "Confidence at deployment: thirty percent. Output at the wound site: ninety. Her class *knows* what it's doing even when she doesn't."

Movement in the corner of Nate's right eye. A second archer. Not a crossbow like his — a shortbow, pulled off the weapons rack in the Safe Room by a wiry woman in a denim jacket. She'd been standing near the back of the support line since the corridor. *Tracker,* Flint said. *Or a Ranger subclass. The System just flagged a marksmanship modifier on her draw — she's getting aim-assist. Call it a laser dot on a ghost sight.* She loosed. The arrow crossed the chamber with the slight wobble of a shaft released from a bow the shooter hadn't fully settled into, but it traveled straight enough — and it bent, in the last five feet, by maybe half a degree. A correction Nate saw and didn't quite believe. The arrow found a Thornling's knothole eye at twenty-five feet and buried itself to the fletching. The Thornling dropped sideways, limbs still reaching, and didn't get back up.

The woman looked at her own bow like it had just lied to her and told the truth at the same time.

Nate logged it. Five classes visible inside the first twelve seconds. Warriors brute-forcing. Mage overspending. Support buffing without knowing. Healer knitting flesh with a beginner's hands. Ranger shooting arrows that corrected themselves mid-flight, because the System was steering the shaft the way it had steered Isabel's swing. Varied downloads. Same cohort. The Tutorial hadn't built one kind of soldier. It had built a *team*, and then it had left out the instruction manual for how to *be* one.

"Six hostiles," Flint said. Still tactical, clipped, the wit dialed back but present enough that Nate could hear it waiting in the wings. "Warriors engaging four. Two breaking east toward the support line. The Mage overspent on that Fire Bolt — her hands are already dimming and the fight's twelve seconds old. At this rate she'll be standing dark in a room full of angry shrubs before the first minute."

Nate raised the crossbow.

---

## III. Crossbow, Rex, Judge

His hands were steady. That surprised him less than it should have — the crossbow was a tool, and tools in his hands had always been steady, whether it was a fencing plier at three in the morning or a rifle on a coyote that had gotten into the chicken pen. The fundamentals transferred: sight picture, breath control, squeeze. The trigger had two degrees of play and he'd already memorized both.

The bolt was a rack bolt — one of the ten mass-produced starter shafts he'd pulled from the wooden box on the Safe Room's supply shelf, the ones nobody else had touched because nobody else had grabbed a crossbow. Plain wooden shaft with a soft-iron tip, basic factory garbage. Ten had come with him out of the starting room. Nine were riding in the belt pouch now. What remained was still garbage. But it was straight, it fit the groove, and the prod's spring steel didn't care what it threw as long as it had something to throw.

Movement on the east side. A Thornling broke through the warrior line, closing fast on a cluster of unarmed Supports backed against the wall.

The peripheral blue flared. Not a thought — a direction. Something in his lower-left visual field brightened, pulling his attention — bird flush, left field. *There.* Before the conscious thought formed, the instinct had already delivered the answer: threat, east wall, closing.

Rex hit it from the side. Not a bite — a darting lunge at the Thornling's legs, thirty-five pounds of Border Collie snapping at bark-covered ankles and cutting back before the thorn-arms could swing. The creature stumbled, turned toward the dog, and Rex was already gone — circling left, nipping at the other side, driving it off-balance — stubborn heifer, meet stubborn dog. Making himself the problem the Thornling had to solve before it could reach anyone else.

The Thornlings didn't spook. No flight instinct to trigger — they weren't cattle, they were constructs, and constructs didn't flinch from a stare or pressure. Rex figured this out in approximately four seconds and adapted. Instead of the patient eye-and-lean that moved cattle through a gate, he was using active harassment — darting lunges, bump-and-break contact, rapid directional changes that kept the Thornling's root-legs guessing. Herding without the herding instinct to exploit. The dog had read the problem and rewritten the playbook on the fly.

Then Judge came in from behind like a forty-pound wrecking ball with opinions.

Where Rex circled, Judge charged straight at the Thornling's trailing root-legs and bit down with the focused aggression of a dog who had never once in her life considered a measured response. The Thornling's legs buckled. It tried to turn — Rex on the left, Judge on the right, neither where they'd been a second ago. Header and heeler. Rex steered it off the fence line. Judge drove it where Rex wanted it to go. The same partnership that had moved a thousand head of cattle through gates they didn't want to enter, applied to a three-and-a-half-foot murder shrub that had even less say in the matter than the cattle.

Its path bent east — away from the Supports, toward open floor. Toward Nate's line of sight. Twenty feet.

Nate squeezed.

The prod snapped and the stock jumped in his hands — just a sharp vibration through the wood and a *thunk* that he felt in his wrists. The bolt crossed twenty feet of torchlit air and hit the Thornling in the left shoulder joint.

Not a kill shot. The bolt punched through bark-plate and sank three inches into whatever served as muscle underneath — dense, fibrous, the color of heartwood. The Thornling staggered sideways, its reaching arm dropping, momentum broken. Amber sap oozed from the wound, thick as honey. It turned toward Nate with a sound like roots tearing from soil, and for one second those dark knotholes that served as eyes locked onto him with the blank focus of a thing deciding whether he was worth killing before it died.

He was already reloading. Second rack bolt seated. Cocked. The prod took the tension — spring steel storing energy, ready to release it.

**Structural Analysis** fired on the staggered Thornling. The bark-plate armor resolved into detail: dense at the chest, thinner at the joints, a seam where the left shoulder plate met the torso plate that was wider than it should be. Growth patterns — compression lines in the bark running parallel to the limb axis, stress concentration at the joint where load-bearing requirements changed direction. Same as checking a fence line after a storm — you didn't need an engineering degree to see where it was about to give. You needed eyes and the patience to look.

He put the second bolt into the seam.

The impact was different this time. The bolt found the gap **Structural Analysis** had highlighted and sank to the fletching — through bark, through the fibrous interior, through whatever passed for a spine in a creature made of wood and spite. The Thornling seized, limbs locking, and collapsed forward onto the stone — a dull, wooden thud. Amber sap pooled beneath it, already darkening.

First kill.

The word sat in his chest with a weight he hadn't expected. Not guilt — he'd killed coyotes and copperheads and one rabid raccoon that had come up the porch steps at 2 AM. Killing a thing that was trying to kill people wasn't a moral question. It was logistics. But this was the first time the thing had been shaped close enough to a person, if you squinted, and the sound it made when it died was close enough to a voice that the difference mattered only because he chose to believe it did.

"First blood," Flint said. If Nate didn't know better, he'd say Flint sounded impressed. "Two bolts, one kill. Your crossbow has a two-degree cant to the left, but for a weapon built from furniture by a man who had ten minutes and no instructions, I'll call it a passing grade. Barely."

---

## IV. The First Chime

The fight was finding its rhythm and its losses at the same time.

On the east wall the warriors had mostly held the line, but "mostly" was doing work that Nate could already see. A Thornling had slipped past Kyle's guard and reached the Support cluster against the north wall — the one Rex and Judge had pulled their Thornling away from. This one was already inside the line.

The man who'd dropped his unfamiliar weapon twice in the opening seconds — a thin-built guy in a brown jacket, no class indicator Nate could read from this distance — had picked it up a third time and was holding it now like a man who'd decided, in his last conscious second, that dying with the thing in his hands was better than dying without it. The Thornling reached him before the Warriors could pivot. A thorn-cluster raked across his throat and chest. He went down without a sound, which was the worst part — the sound was what his body had meant to make. It just didn't get there.

Ana Torres was moving toward him before he hit the stone. Too far. Too late.

A second Thornling on the west side punched through a seam in the line that a stumbling mace swing had opened and laid an unarmed woman open at the ribs. She made the sound the man hadn't. Two more seconds and there was a third — a Support in the rear who'd turned to see what was happening on the west side and caught a thorn-arm across the shoulder that severed something important. He collapsed against the wall and slid down it, trying to stand back up and not managing.

Three dead in about six seconds. The warrior line closed the gaps it had opened, but closing them was a different thing than preventing them.

Isabel Wu — the Warrior whose forearm Ana had just healed — roared.

It wasn't a word. It was the sound a person made when their body decided the lesson had been loud enough and the next Thornling was going to pay the bill for three of them. She stepped forward into the Thornling that had killed the unarmed woman, and her technique was still Rank 1 Low — still rough, still scared — except this time it wasn't. This time her body believed it. Her first swing caught the Thornling on the thigh, split bark, sank deep. Her second came down on its shoulder and cleaved the arm at the joint. Her third took off the head.

A golden pulse flared around her for less than a second — a chime Nate couldn't hear but could see in the way her stance shifted and her mouth opened in a small *oh*. Flint's glitchy combat log flickered — fragments of data from a network Flint shouldn't have been reading: **[SWORD TECHNIQUE — RANK 1: LOW → MID. ENHANCED OPTION UNLOCKED: POWER STRIKE.]**

Her next swing was different. Faster, tighter, the blade glowing faintly as the System guided it into the seam of a Thornling closing from her left. The first System-assisted strike Nate had seen — not just downloaded knowledge, but active guidance, the System steering her arm through the motion. It hit harder than anything she'd thrown before. The Thornling dropped before it had time to reset.

Her stance settled. That was what Nate noticed. Even in the middle of the chamber, with amber sap drying on her forearm and three bodies on the stone behind her, Isabel Wu's weight had redistributed between her feet. The System had decided it liked the new stance. Her shoulders had dropped half an inch. Her grip had loosened. The kind of adjustment a body made when it had just been told, at the deep-tissue level that didn't take suggestions from the conscious mind, that a thing it had been doing wrong for two minutes it was now officially doing right.

She was already looking for the next target with the hungry focus of someone who wanted to feel that chime a second time.

*And there it is.* The loop. The reward architecture laid bare. Skill milestone — her basic sword technique had ticked from Low to Mid within Rank 1, not even a full rank-up, just a sub-tier, and the System gave her a golden chime and a light show for it. Nate had seen this exact design in a dozen games. Reward the smallest progress with the biggest fanfare. Make the click feel good. Make the player chase the next click.

It worked in games. It was working here too. And the difference between a game reward loop and this — this was the difference between a slot machine and a surgery. Both modified behavior. Only one of them cut.

"I caught a packet," Flint said internally, tight. Working register. No jokes. "That was a skill-tier event. The System watched her kill rate, her technique consistency, her confidence under pressure, and it cashed her out with a chime. If the spec sheet I can read is any guide, every fighter in this room is being graded in real time. The ones who trust the download get the next tier first."

On the west side, Heather threw her second Fire Bolt. Weaker than the first. Noticeably. The first had been a bonfire. The second was a campfire. Mana cost catching up with enthusiasm. The Thornling she'd aimed at was already on fire from the first bolt and dropped anyway, but the math was on a clock and the clock was not her friend.

And the teenager — Pete Bowman — watched Isabel's chime land with the specific terror of someone who had just watched another fighter unlock something he couldn't reach. No golden light for him. No enhanced option. His technique was still Rank 1 Low and the Thornling in front of him was not going to wait for him to figure out why.

Pete's next swing was late. He caught it halfway, corrected on the fly, and got the shortsword up in time to block a thorn-arm that would otherwise have taken him across the face. Nate saw the exact second the kid understood that watching the chime had nearly killed him. The block shuddered through Pete's elbow; his knees bent and held; he stepped back and reset and came in again with a thrust that landed flat in the bark-plate. Not pretty. But seated. No chime for Pete. Not yet. The System had watched him watch Isabel and had decided, in whatever way the System decided such things, that hesitation was not the currency it paid out milestones for.

Isabel's next swing took that Thornling too.

---

## V. Four Dead

The last two Thornlings went down in the next ten seconds.

Kyle and the Mace woman — Mack, Nate was now ninety percent sure of the name — converged on the fifth and brute-forced it with more aggression than coordination. Pete and a second shield-bearer trapped the sixth against the east wall and Isabel closed the distance in three steps, Power Strike glowing faintly on the blade, and put it down the way a practiced ranch hand finished a hog.

The chamber went quiet.

Not silent. Silence would have required the absence of breathing, and there was none of that. What it went was *still* — the specific post-combat stillness where everyone was alive enough to be aware that some people weren't. Amber sap pooling on the stone. Mineral dust settling. Torchlight dimming and rising as the draft found its balance. A Healer crying — not Ana, a different one, kneeling at the ribs of the woman who'd been unarmed and was now the second body. A man sitting down against the wall, hard, not looking at anyone. Kyle Greene still holding the longsword like he was afraid to put it down.

Four dead on the cohort side. Nate counted them because counting was what he did.

The jacket man, the unarmed woman at the west wall, the Support slumped against the stone at the rear. And a fourth Nate hadn't seen drop — a woman near the east corner, a thorn through the side below the ribs, already gone by the time anyone reached her. She was tall and her hair was gray at the temples and Nate hadn't learned her name.

Nobody had. That was what he registered last, and hardest. He'd been in the corridor with them less than two hours ago. They hadn't had time to trade names. The Tutorial had ensured that most of the introductions were posthumous.

*Four names the Tutorial will not be assigning homework to. The Storyteller's list grows — sure, it grew before you came in here, love, and it will grow after. But the ones in this chamber just went on it newly, and the room knows.*

Ana Torres moved between the bodies with the careful hands of a woman whose white light was working and whose white light could not work on this. She closed the jacket man's eyes. The other Healer — younger, shaking — was trying to close the unarmed woman's and couldn't find the shape to do it. Ana reached over and did that too. Nobody told them to do it. The dead had been dying for less than three minutes and the living had already decided on a ceremony.

Judge stood at Nate's right knee, staring at the dissolving Thornling corpses with the pointed intensity of a dog who took the continued existence of enemies as a personal insult. When the corpses finished dissolving, she looked at Nate and sighed — a dramatic, full-body exhale that communicated, clearly and without ambiguity, that she had been having the time of her life and he had let it end too soon.

Rex set at his left. Not panting. Watching the south wall — because that was the next spawn direction, and he had decided, apparently, that the job was not done until someone gave him the all-clear.

Nate's hand found Rex's head and got a flash — the Thornling's legs from six inches off the ground, the angle of its stumble, the exact moment Rex had broken off because the threat was past. A thirty-second combat engagement compressed into a single image, delivered through warm fur under Nate's palm. Urgency fading to pack-satisfaction. The job was done.

"Yeah, you did," Nate murmured. Rex's ear flicked toward his voice — not the working ear, the other one. The one that was always listening for Nate even when the rest of him was on the job.

"Six hostiles, four cohort fatalities," Flint said, and the combat edge was gone from his voice. Not replaced by his usual theater — replaced by something quieter. "The warriors performed adequately, which is a word I'm using generously. The **Mage** needs a fuel management seminar. The **Healer** who healed the arm saved a Warrior who later killed three Thornlings, which is a data point I am going to archive under 'leverage.' And your crossbow—"

"Two-degree cant. I know."

"I was going to mention the trigger delay. But since you're running your own diagnostic, I'll just sit here and feel professionally redundant."

The wit was dialed low. Dialed to the room. Flint had read the stone floor and the ceremony and had adjusted.

---

## VI. Forty-Six

The south wall groaned.

Not a collapse. A release — the stone-grind of a Tutorial door that had been closed thirty seconds ago and had just received the signal to open. A seam resolved in the south wall, widening from a hairline to a gap wide enough for two people abreast, and warmer light bled through from the corridor beyond. Not the torchlit dim of the combat rooms. Steady amber. The kind of light you lit rooms with when you wanted people to breathe in them.

Rest Area 1.

Marcus moved first, the way he always moved first — putting his body between the group and the next unknown without announcing it. He stepped up to the gap, looked through, came back. "It's clear. Workbench on the far wall. Healers can work in there. Let's move the wounded first."

People started to move. Slow at first. Then faster. A Healer and two Warriors carried Kyle's limp-armed Mace partner Mack, who'd taken a thorn through the calf in the last ten seconds and was walking badly. Ana stayed at the bodies for another beat. Just long enough to confirm that the stone was going to take them back into itself the way it had taken the Thornlings. Slower, though — the Tutorial was apparently willing to grant that cohort dead and Tutorial monsters had different dissolution timers. Then she moved through after them.

Nate waited. He wanted to be last through. He didn't have a good reason. It was just what he did when the herd was moving through a gate — you didn't go until the stragglers went, because the stragglers were the ones who decided at the last second that they weren't going at all.

Rex had positioned himself six feet off Nate's hip, facing the gap, reading the people going through it like they were cattle he was personally responsible for. He wasn't leaving until the last person cleared the threshold. That was not a decision he'd put up for a vote.

Judge had found a thorn on the floor — an unsorted one Josie hadn't gotten to yet — and was considering whether it now belonged to her. She was losing the argument. The thorn was oozing amber sap, and Judge's nose had just discovered what amber sap smelled like up close. Her face worked through three distinct stages of dog-disappointment in about two seconds. She left the thorn. She gave it a look on the way past that suggested she and the thorn would be revisiting the conversation at a later date.

Josie Pickett — who had spent the combat with her back against the north wall and her eyes open, processing everything and running toward none of it — was already at the Thornling remains, crouched, examining. Not a Warrior, not a Mage, not a Healer. An **Appraiser**, if her Safe Room roll had held, and she was appraising. Nate filed that for the rest area. Josie would not be sitting anything out.

He stepped toward the gap.

Judge went first, because Judge went first wherever there was a door. Rex paced Nate on his left. At the threshold, Nate paused and looked back once — one slow sweep across the chamber. The amber pools where the Thornlings had been, now darkening. The four patches where the cohort dead had been, now empty stone. The torches dim. The south wall wide open and bleeding warm light into a dark room.

The Tutorial had lessons. This one had cost four.

*Fifty walked into this room. Four of them did not walk out, mind. The Tutorial watches them go through the gap one by one, and says nothing — because there is nothing useful to say to forty-six people who do not yet know that the number they are leaving in this chamber is smaller than the number waiting in the next one.*

"Forty-six," Nate said, soft enough that only Flint heard it.

"Four fewer than entered," Flint answered.

Rex walked through the gap ahead of him. Nate followed.

For a half-step the chamber held him anyway — the way a doorway sometimes did, when what was behind you had weight the next room didn't. The smell of amber sap. The dim torchlight. The faint scrape of Thornling remains still going back into the stone in the south corners. The chamber's dust, mostly settled now, rising again at the edges where the air pressure shifted through the opening door. Nate had been on exactly one battlefield before this. Twenty-three years ago, in a training exercise outside Fort Hood that had gone wrong in a way that killed one man and put two more in hospitals. What he remembered about that chamber, after, was that the air had tasted the same on the way out as it had on the way in. Nobody had ever written that down in any after-action report. Nobody had a register for it.

This chamber tasted different on the way out. That was the part he'd remember.

He stepped through.

The stone-grind of the door closing behind them was the loudest sound the chamber had made since the scratching stopped.
