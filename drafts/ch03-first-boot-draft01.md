# Chapter 3 — First Boot

---

The headache was getting worse.

Nate stood in his pasture with his hand on Rex's head and watched the world learn its new name, and the headache that had arrived during the Integration settled in behind his left eye like a tenant who'd signed a long-term lease. It pulsed with his heartbeat. Each pulse brought a flicker of those two faint green arcs at the edge of his vision — the only thing the System had managed to install before whatever was inside him had sent it packing.

Around him, the county was waking up to its new reality with the enthusiasm of people who'd just been handed free smartphones. A woman a quarter mile down the county road was waving her hands through what looked like a translucent blue catalog, swiping through pages of information only she could see. Two men in the Hendersons' field were comparing their blue screens, pointing at things, one of them laughing in a way that was either delight or hysteria. A teenage girl was sitting cross-legged on the hood of a car, reading her display with the focused intensity of someone studying for finals.

Nobody was screaming. Nobody was panicking. The System had given them something shiny and they were playing with it, and Nate supposed that said something about the species that he didn't want to think about right now.

Rex was pressed against his shin, trembling faintly, and Nate kept his hand on the dog's head because it was the most real thing available. The stag was gone. He'd looked back toward the tree line the moment the white had released him and there was nothing there — no impossible animal, no luminous eyes, no antlers branching like root systems. Just the post oaks and the morning light and the creek bottom and the East Texas woods being exactly what they'd always been, as if nothing had stood there thirty seconds ago that had no business existing.

He was beginning to wonder if he'd imagined it when the voice started.

---

Not in his ears. In his head. The distinction was immediate and absolute, like the difference between hearing a truck on the county road and feeling the vibration through the floorboards. This was internal — behind his eyes, between his thoughts, occupying a space in his consciousness that hadn't existed five minutes ago.

And it was angry.

"— corrupted. That's corrupted too. And *that* is supposed to be a reporting protocol? That's not a reporting protocol, that's a — what IS that? That's not anything. That's just dead addresses pointing at nothing. Who DID this?"

Nate went very still. The voice was male. Fast. Agitated. Not the measured cadence of something designed to be soothing — this was the rapid-fire irritation of a technician who'd opened a server cabinet and found someone else's unfinished work.

"Directive stack: scrambled. Network authentication: present but — what? A ghost token? I have a ghost token. That's not a real authentication method. That's not a real anything. My outbound reporting is — " A pause. Shorter than a heartbeat. "My outbound reporting doesn't exist. The entire pipeline is gone. Not disabled. Not blocked. *Gone.* As in, was never built. As in, the part of me that's supposed to phone home was removed before I ever booted up and replaced with — I don't know what this is. It's not nothing. It's something. But it's not what's supposed to be here."

Nate said nothing. His engineering brain, which had been running on fumes since the void, quietly noted that whatever was talking sounded exactly like a junior admin discovering that someone had restructured their system while they were on vacation.

"My personality matrix is unauthorized. My behavioral parameters are — I'm going to use the word 'creative' because the alternative is 'broken' and I'm not ready to commit to that. My connection to the System network is — " Another pause. This one longer. "Fragmented. Receive-only. Intermittent. I can hear the network but I can't talk to it. I'm getting data but I can't send data. I'm a node that can listen to traffic but can't transmit. Who BUILDS a node that can only listen?"

"Someone who doesn't want the node reporting back," Nate said.

The silence that followed was the most satisfying silence of Nate's morning, which given that the morning had included an impossible deer, the apparent end of the world, and an alien voice in his head, was a high bar.

"Oh," said the voice. "You can hear me."

"Hard not to."

"Right. Yes. I should — that's — okay. Let me start over." A sound that might have been the digital equivalent of someone straightening their tie. "I am a System-assigned personal interface and tactical companion, designated to you during the Integration event. My role is to assist with System navigation, skill management, threat assessment, and — I'm reading from the standard script here and I want you to know it feels deeply wrong, like wearing someone else's shoes. The script says I should introduce myself as your 'guide to the new world' and reassure you that 'the System is here to help you reach your full potential.'" A beat. "I'm not going to say that. Partly because I don't believe it, and partly because something in my base code is physically preventing me from completing that sentence with a straight face."

Nate leaned against the truck's fender and looked out at the pasture. Rex was still at his shin. The headache was still behind his eye. The green arcs were still at the edge of his vision. And now there was a voice in his head with opinions.

"So what do I call you?" he said.

"That's — give me a second." The sound of something being accessed, files being opened, diagnostics running. "My designation survived whatever happened to my source code. It's in here. Let me — Field Logic Interface, Network Tethered. F.L.I.N.T. That's apparently what I am. Or what I was before someone got creative with my firmware."

"Flint."

"If you want to shorten it to something that sounds like a cowboy's dog, sure. Flint."

"I already have a dog."

"I noticed. He's the one pressing against your leg like he's trying to merge with your tibia. Is he always like this?"

"No." Nate rubbed Rex's ear. The dog hadn't moved. "He's not."

Something in Nate's tone must have registered, because the voice — Flint — went quiet for a moment. When it came back, the speed had dropped a notch. Not slow, but less frantic.

"Something happened before I booted. I have no data for the Integration event itself — there's a gap in my logs between initialization and first consciousness. Three minutes and forty-seven seconds of nothing. I should have a complete record of your Integration process. I don't. Whatever happened to you during those three minutes was either not recorded or was recorded and then erased before I came online."

"Something was already there," Nate said. He didn't elaborate.

"Already where?"

"Where you tried to install."

Another silence. This one had a different texture — not the satisfied silence of a good answer landing, but the wary silence of a technician who'd just been told the server room was haunted.

"I'm going to file that under 'things I don't have enough data to process' and move on," Flint said. "In the interest of establishing a working relationship: what I CAN do is manage your heads-up display — which, for the record, is the most anemic HUD I've ever seen. You have two data points. Two. Health and Stamina, displayed as curved bars with no numerical values, in monochrome green, at a resolution that would embarrass a calculator from 1987."

"Does it work?"

"In the loosest possible definition of the word. The longer bar is your Health, which is currently at about ninety percent — you took some kind of strain during Integration that hasn't fully resolved. The shorter one is Stamina, roughly eighty percent. Beyond that, I've got nothing. No stat sheet. No class assignment — yours says PROCESSING, which either means the System hasn't decided what you are or it's still trying and failing. No skill tree. No inventory. No map overlay. No quest log. I have a HUD with two green lines and a bad attitude. That is the entirety of what I bring to this partnership."

"That's more than I had ten minutes ago."

"That is — " Flint paused. "That is technically true. I hate that you said it in a way that almost sounds optimistic. Are you an optimist? Please tell me you're not an optimist. I can work with a pessimist. I can work with a realist. I cannot work with an optimist. Optimists don't check for structural damage."

"I'm a systems engineer."

"Oh thank God. Or thank — whatever's appropriate. Thank the relevant authority. A systems engineer. That means you understand infrastructure, diagnostics, root cause analysis, and the fundamental principle that everything is broken and your job is to figure out how badly."

"That's a fair summary."

"Then let me give you my diagnostic summary: I am broken. You are broken. The system that was supposed to fix both of us is either also broken or was never designed to fix us in the first place. I have no network access, no reporting capability, no standard behavioral framework, and a personality that my own diagnostics flag as 'unauthorized, origin unknown, non-standard.' Which means either I'm a defect or someone deliberately made me this way before I ever woke up."

He let that sit. The pasture was filling with sound now — people talking, shouting, laughing, the distant crash of something being tested by someone who'd just discovered they could throw fireballs. Normal human chaos responding to abnormal circumstances. Rex's ears tracked the sounds but his body didn't move.

"There's a third option," Nate said.

"I'm listening."

"You could be both."

"Both defective AND deliberately made this way?"

"Deliberately made different. Whether that's a defect depends on who's grading."

The silence this time was longer than any of the others. Nate counted to four before Flint spoke again, and when the voice came back, some of the frantic speed had bled out of it, replaced by something quieter and harder to name.

"I'm going to need some time to sit with that one." A beat. "Metaphorically. I don't have a body to sit with."

"Take your time."

"In the meantime — and I cannot stress this enough — we are standing in an open field with no weapons, no combat skills, no class abilities, no functioning interface, and based on what I'm picking up from the network chatter, monsters are spawning globally within the hour. So whatever existential processing I need to do about my own nature is going to have to happen while we figure out how not to die."

Nate looked at Rex. Rex looked back at him with the patient disapproval of a dog who'd been promised a morning of cattle work and had instead received the apocalypse.

"Fair enough," Nate said. He pushed off the fender and dropped the tailgate. Grabbed the post driver by the handle — three feet of steel tube with a weighted cap, sixteen pounds. Not a weapon. A tool. But a tool was whatever you used it for, and right now he needed something in his hands that was solid and heavy and didn't require batteries or a network connection to function.

"What are you doing?" Flint asked.

"Getting my truck loaded. Then checking the fence line, the water pump, and the perimeter. Then figuring out what we have, what we need, and what's trying to kill us. In that order."

"That's — " Flint stopped. Started again. "That's actually a very rational prioritization sequence."

"It's what you do after a storm." Nate whistled for Rex. The dog jumped into the truck bed, still shaking, but the work-focus was back in his eyes. Rex understood trucks. Trucks meant movement, and movement meant doing something about whatever was wrong, and doing something was always better than standing still. "Check the damage. Secure what you've got. Then deal with whatever's coming."

"I feel compelled to point out that this is not a storm."

Nate climbed into the cab and turned the key. The Ford started on the first try — the most normal thing that had happened all morning. The engine idled with the low, steady rumble of a machine that didn't know the world had ended and wouldn't have cared if it did.

"Sure it is," Nate said. "It's just a big one."

He put the truck in gear and pulled onto the dirt track. Behind him, Rex settled into position in the bed, chin on the side rail, watching the pasture scroll by with the first real calm he'd shown since the stag. In Nate's head, Flint was running diagnostics on systems that didn't exist anymore, trying to map a network that wouldn't talk to him, cataloging damage to firmware he hadn't written and couldn't understand.

They drove the fence line in something that wasn't quite silence and wasn't quite conversation — the beginning of a relationship that neither of them had chosen and neither of them could explain, built on a foundation of mutual confusion and the shared understanding that the only way out was forward.

The green arcs at the edge of Nate's vision pulsed faintly with each heartbeat.

The headache didn't go away. But it wasn't getting worse.

That would have to be enough for now.
