# Chapter 7 — Cover Positions

---

Room 3 was where the Tutorial stopped asking and started telling, mind.

But first the corridor widened.

---

The torch-light changed first — from the combat chamber's flicker to the steady amber burn that meant infrastructure, not exertion. Then the ceiling lifted. Then the walls smoothed from rough-cut to dressed stone, and the corridor opened into the second rest area of the Tutorial: warmer, wider, better-lit.

Better-stocked, too. Against the west wall, the crafting station had grown teeth. A grinder wheel. A small anvil on a thick iron base. Three tiers of files graded fine-to-coarse. A bench vise screwed to a plank that had been set into the stone floor so solid that **Structural Analysis** read it as permanent architecture — not generated props, not placed hardware, but fixtures the **System** intended to stay.

Reward scaling. Classic design. He'd seen it in every MMO rest zone he'd ever bunked down in. Better crafting tools with each progression tier, and never the crafting tools you actually wanted up front — always the ones you'd earned by grinding through the previous floor. Engineer's version of dopamine, delivered in the shape of a good bench vise.

Forty-three entered. The math nobody had voiced since the door closed behind them. Two combat rooms behind them, seven bodies on the stone of rooms that had already started to feel like memory. Nate sat at the workbench and let the number settle — not arithmetic, not yet, but a shape he was still getting used to holding.

Rex set against his left boot. Not lying down — set, the way a working dog parked weight the moment the task paused, three points of contact with the floor and the ribs rising and falling against the denim of Nate's pants. Ears up, eyes half-lidded, the specific alertness of a dog who had decided the corridor was secure and was therefore downshifting into companion mode rather than off-duty mode. Rex didn't do off-duty.

Nate rested a hand behind Rex's ears. Through the contact came something warm and uncomplicated — not a picture exactly, but the feel of a thing: the porch at dusk, the creak of the swing, the porch rail still warm from the afternoon sun. Rex's version of *home*, shared without effort, the kind of transmission that in the old days Nate would have called imagination and in the new days he'd stopped needing to explain.

"We'll get back there," Nate said, quiet.

Rex's tail moved once. Slow. Certain.

Judge flopped against his other thigh with the theatrical finality of a dog who had personally won every fight in the Tutorial so far and intended to be thanked for it. She rolled onto her back, four paws in the air, and fixed the ceiling with a glare that said she was *also* tired and *also* here and *at least* as load-bearing as that other dog over there. After three seconds of display, she rolled upright, set her head against Nate's thigh, and closed one eye. The other eye stayed open on Rex.

*The rest areas look like kindness. And they are — in the way a watering trough is kindness. Water when you're thirsty, shade when you're hot, everything the herd needs to keep moving through the pasture the herdsman has already mapped. Kindness inside a design is still design, love. The trough isn't the end. It's the reason the herd walks another mile.*

Across the corridor, Josie Pickett had set up a supply chain.

Not a metaphor — an actual, working, three-station sorting operation with bark-plate fragments graded by laminate quality, thorn tips separated by curvature, and root-fiber bundled into hank lengths a crafter could actually use. The leather-worker from Rest Area 1 was back at her station. So was a tinkerer Nate didn't recognize. So was a weaver who had clearly not expected to discover, in her fifties, that the **System** had handed her a class whose ability list included *fibrous structural integration.*

"The Room 3 stock is better," Josie was explaining to the weaver, holding up a bark-plate fragment with the calm certainty of a woman running an estate sale. "See the interlocking grain? Three-layer laminate, stress-tested, combat-grade. Room 1 fragments were practice. *These* are the real deal."

The weaver felt the fragment. Nodded. Handed Josie something small wrapped in cloth in exchange — Nate couldn't tell what from his angle — and went back to her bench with the air of a person who had just made a trade she did not yet fully understand but was certain had been fair.

"She's running cross-station inventory," Flint said. "By memory. For three materials and four crafters. I am continuing to find this more unsettling than impressive, and I want the record to reflect that I understand the difference."

"You keep a record of what unsettles you?"

"I keep a record of *everything*, Nate. Being unsettled is just the column I flag in red."

Marcus drifted into the workbench's range with the careful approach of a man who was always gathering data even when he looked like he was just walking.

"Headcount," he said.

"Forty-three."

"That's the one." He glanced toward the corridor that led back to Room 2, then to the one that led forward to Room 3 — a quick, lateral read of ingress and egress that was pure Marcus. "Ramp's real. Room 1 was a whiff test. Room 2 was a resource check. I'd put a hundred on Room 3 being something we haven't seen yet."

"Hundred dollars?"

"Hundred dollars I don't have, because my wallet's on the nightstand in a house I may never see again. But the figure stands."

Nate almost smiled. Almost. The Tutorial had a way of flattening humor before it reached the face. Still — the shape of the exchange was the shape of two men sizing up the next pasture, and three rooms in, that was not nothing.

---

The crossbow was off.

Not broken — the prod sat seated, the stock had held through three rooms of use, the trigger mechanism still locked and released with the clean bite of well-fitted wood. But the sight picture was wrong. At twenty feet, the two-degree left cant of the prod was manageable by feel. At forty — Room 3's projected distance, if Marcus's instinct was right — *feel* was a variable Nate wanted to retire.

He pulled the crossbow flat on the bench.

A torch bracket protruded from the wall at bench height — iron, rectangular, set into a groove carved to hold it. **Structural Analysis** read it in the same glance: carbon content consistent with low-grade wrought iron, cross-section two inches by a quarter, work-hardening visible along the top edge from whatever had shaped it in the first place. It would file into a ring with about eight minutes of patient work.

Nate pried it off the wall. The **System's** generated fixtures came free cleaner than Earth-side ones — no stubborn anchor bolts, no decades of paint fused into the mounting holes. One steady pull and the bracket was his.

He clamped it in the vise. Picked up the medium file. Started cutting.

Hands-busy work. The best kind. Brain ran while the hands filed, and the hands filed while the brain ran, and the whole process moved forward because neither half had to wait on the other.

The **Structural Analysis** wireframe held steady on the metal as he worked. Cleaner than it had been at the Rest Area 1 bench — the blue lines resolving with less flicker, the stress points drawing in tighter as each pass of the file changed the cross-section. Metal was talking back. Not in words, but in the way the surface registered each bite: fresh-cut grain here, work-hardened zone there, a hairline cold-shut running lateral near the base that he hadn't seen until the third pass exposed it.

"**Structural Analysis**: Resolution Tier 2," Flint said, conversational. "Forty-two percent. Material-history parsing is online — you're reading stress-lines at a depth the wireframe wasn't rendering in Room 1."

Nate didn't pause. He kept filing.

"Translated," Flint added, "you're starting to see the metal's biography. Not just its current shape — its life. Where it was forged, where it got hit, where the grain fought the forming. On a peep sight, that's overkill. On something that matters later, it'll earn its keep."

"What changed?"

"Use. You've been running **Structural Analysis** continuously across three combat rooms and a crafting bench. The channel trains itself by running. If the spec sheet I can read is any guide, Tier 3 is another significant jump — but that's a Tier 3 problem, and we're not there yet."

Fifteen more strokes. The center of the bracket opened into a ring. Nate checked it against the crossbow rail, marked the friction-fit tolerance with a thumbnail scratch, filed twenty seconds more. Checked again. Fit.

Mounted it.

**Field Repair** bonded the iron ring to the wooden rail with the quiet permanence of materials properly introduced — not glue, not a fastener, just the kind of joint that held because two materials had been shown where they belonged.

Sight picture through the ring: clean. The two-degree cant was still there — the prod's native bias, filed into the weapon's bones from the original jig — but now Nate could see it. Measure it. Compensate for it consistently instead of guessing by feel each shot.

Engineering didn't eliminate imperfection. It made imperfection predictable.

"Rough error reduction, forty percent at distance," Flint said. "I'm using the word *meaningful* because *adequate* felt insulting. Note that. I plan to cite it later when you ignore my advice about something."

"You keep a ledger of favors?"

"Favors I track separately. Compound interest, Nate — you're already in arrears, and you don't even know the rate."

Two benches down, Walt Keane glanced up from a bark-plate he was working. His eye slid over the peep sight — slow, appraising, the sale-barn squint of a man who had spent thirty years sizing up other people's livestock and never lost the habit. He grunted once. Approving. The way an auctioneer's *fair enough* settled a price.

Coming from Walt, that was a paragraph.

---

The door opened.

Not the crack-and-grind of Room 2's entry — this one eased, stone on stone, the kind of opening a foundry uses when the work inside wants to breathe. Warm air pushed through with a smell Nate didn't have a name for yet: scorched sap with something greener underneath, and beneath that, the dry mineral scent of real stone that had been cut a long time ago and never weathered.

Rex lifted off his boot in a motion that did not look like getting up so much as reassembly — ears first, head second, the rest of him in a single fluid stack. Judge was already up. Ears pinned. Weight forward, stub tail rigid. She stared into the dark beyond the doorway with the personal offense of a dog who had not been introduced to whatever was down there and intended to correct that oversight.

They walked in.

Room 3 was sixty feet across. Not a cube — a chamber. Natural rock formations broke the floor into corridors and cover positions. The east wall rose into a three-tier rock shelf that gave elevation and sight lines to the center. A choke point narrowed between two stone ridges on the north side, four feet wide at the pinch, wide enough for two men shoulder-to-shoulder and no wider. The west wall held a pair of flanking corridors behind knee-high rock formations — passages that funneled anything moving through them into tight, one-exit paths.

**Structural Analysis** ran the geometry and came back with a reading Nate hadn't expected.

Not generated stone. Quarried. Quarried and set. The tool marks on the cover formations were old — real-rock old, the kind that took centuries to settle into the patina they were wearing. The east shelf had been built. The choke point had been cut. Whoever had designed Room 3 had designed it, and then someone had *built* it, and then enough time had passed that the rock remembered being cut more clearly than it remembered being placed.

"The room is not a simulation," Flint said. Quieter than usual. "Not in the sense the first two were. I don't know what to do with that yet. But I'd like the record to note that I said it first."

Eight spawn points cycled along the perimeter in the stagger-pattern Nate had learned to read: two, pause, three, pause, three. The familiar rhythm.

A ninth spawn point, dead center of the room, pulsed on a different clock. Slower. Deeper. The kind of warming-up cycle that belonged to a machine building pressure for something heavier than its neighbors were about to deliver.

The first eight fired.

Thornlings — eight of them, bark-plated, root-legged, thorn-armed, the same combat dummies Rooms 1 and 2 had taught the cohort to kill. They spread into the room in the rough fan that said the spawn system had learned the cohort's positioning and was trying to compensate.

Then the ninth fired.

The proto-guard grew into the room the way a tree would grow in fast-forward — roots first, then trunk, then the spread of the upper structure, all of it compressing a season into six seconds of visible assembly. Seven feet tall at the shoulder. Bark-plate armor twice as thick as a basic Thornling's, layered and overlapping and compressed at the chest into something load-bearing rather than decorative. Arms that ended in thorn clusters so dense they looked braided — the kind of clusters that would shred chain and not notice the difference. No eyes that registered as eyes. Two knotholes on its head that tracked across the room and read positions.

Eighteen hundred pounds of bad attitude deciding where to charge.

Nate had met that math before. Congressman — his registered Red Angus herd bull, eighteen-hundred-plus when the hay was good — had the same look when the vet rig pulled into the drive. Except Congressman eventually calmed down when you scratched behind his ears.

This thing didn't have ears.

"New," Flint said. "And by new I mean: if the spec sheet I can read is any guide, significantly worse for us than the baseline Thornlings. Bigger, denser, the armor-plate geometry suggests coordinated movement. I'd put my assessment at *a draft that's been in revision.* Not the final version of what it's turning into. What happens when the basic Thornlings file a complaint and the **System** adjusts."

**Structural Analysis** fired hard — harder than it had fired on anything since the crossbow. Density, yes. But density had structure, and structure had seams. The arm joints were thicker than the basic Thornlings' but the growth pattern was the same: bark plates overlapping at the joints, narrower seams but present. The shoulder-to-torso connection showed a gap where the heavier armor hadn't fused completely — load-bearing growth that hadn't caught up to the creature's final geometry. Chest plate too thick. Joints exposed. Same shape, drafted heavier.

Whatever it was growing into, it wasn't finished. The seams were still open.

---

Marcus was already calling it.

"**Warriors** hold the choke!" His voice flat-edged, command-voice, the tone of a man who had issued orders his whole working life and expected them obeyed before they finished landing. "Two-man front. Rotate on cooldown. Sam — you take lead beat. Mack — cover. *Rotate.*" Then east, up toward the shelf: "Heather — elevation. Line of sight to center. Burst fire, then *wait.* Let mana regen. You've got four bolts in you before you're dark. Use three. Hold one." He lifted three fingers. "Three. Not four."

Heather Kim nodded once from the ledge. Her hands were already at her knees, palms cupped, mana banking slow and deliberate instead of flaring the way it had in Rooms 1 and 2. The look on her face was the look of a twenty-five-year-old who had burned dry once and did not intend to burn dry again.

"**Healer** — behind the line, do *not* advance. If the fight moves to you, back up. Let them come to your range."

Tank. Healer. Damage.

Nate watched the shape of it form without naming it. He'd seen the shape in a hundred games and in more tabletop sessions than he'd admit at a Sunday cookout. Three roles, each covering the other two's weakness, the geometry that held when every other geometry fell apart. Marcus wasn't running it because he'd read it in a book. He was running it because he'd been running the same shape in every operation he'd ever led, and the **System** had given him the vocabulary to make it visible.

The warriors took the choke.

Sam Hargrove and Mack Turner, shoulder-to-shoulder in the four-foot pinch between the stone ridges. The rock formations did the teaching. A Thornling coming through had to pass the two-man front to reach anything behind it, and the two-man front was a dance now — Sam's base technique on the lead beat, Mack's **Power Strike** on the cover, then swap, then again. Each of them had earned a milestone chime in the previous room. Neither of them was good. Both of them were *adequate*, and adequate, multiplied by rotation, was a wall.

A Thornling clipped Sam's shin. Bark-thorn across the calf, hot line of blood through torn pants. Sam grunted. Didn't drop. His blade kept coming on the upswing because the **System** was carrying his arm through the motion and the **System** did not care that he had just been hit. Behind him, Mack stepped up with her **Power Strike** primed, and Sam rotated back breathing hard, hand pressed to the cut, eyes counting the cooldown seconds.

*Sure, two rough men swapping the lead beat with the metronome rhythm of dancers who have no idea they are dancing. They will never use the word. But their feet know it, mind. And in the room's acoustics, for those eight seconds, the choke sounds like music.*

Heather cast her first **Fire Bolt**.

Controlled. Tight beam. Less mana in the cast than she had spent on any single bolt in Room 2. The shot traveled in a focused line instead of flaring, hit a basic Thornling center-mass, dropped it clean. She lowered her hands. Closed her eyes. Banked regen.

Discipline. Room 2's lesson paid forward.

The cohort was watching her as much as the fight. Not with words — with the corner-eye attention of people who had seen her go dry once and had understood, wordless, that the woman on the ledge was the reason the choke was still holding. Three bolts, not four. That was the deal. The math on her elevated position had become the cohort's math.

Nate took the shelf two positions east of Heather. Higher ground. Crossbow resting across a rock shelf that **Structural Analysis** had already measured as stable — flat surface, correct height, natural shooting rest, perfect for what he was about to do. The peep sight caught the torchlight as he sighted through it.

"Nate." Marcus's voice, direct. "The big one. Center. Tell me what you see."

---

**Structural Analysis** was already reading the proto-guard.

The creature had paused at the center of the room. Not confused — assessing. The knothole eyes moved across the warrior line, the elevated **Mage**, the **Healer** position. A predator reading a herd. Looking for the gap.

"Arm joints are the weakness," Nate said. "Bark plate at the chest is too thick for anything short of Heather burning dry. But the joints overlap with narrow seams. Left shoulder has a growth gap — the armor didn't fuse where the load-bearing direction changes. That's the entry."

**Structural Analysis** painted more across his vision — the south-wall spawn points warming, the rhythm of the ninth spawn going quiet now that the proto-guard was loose. "And south-wall spawn fires again in about thirty seconds. Three incoming."

"**Warriors** — south wall, thirty seconds!" Marcus relayed without breaking stride. A man who had spent a career turning field intel into fire orders did not need to understand **Structural Analysis** to trust the source. "Nate — the big one is yours. Slow it down. Don't kill it. *Pin* it. We finish when the adds are clear."

Marcus read people. Nate read architecture. Between them, they had the room.

Nate sighted on the proto-guard's left shoulder.

The gap was two inches wide. At forty feet, with a handmade crossbow that had a two-degree left cant he was now compensating for *by measurement* instead of feel — thanks to an iron ring filed from a torch bracket — the shot was challenging but not desperate. On the ranch, the rifle version of this was dropping a coyote through brush at fifty yards with a rifle that pulled left and a light wind he hadn't fully read. He had made that shot on Tuesdays.

He released.

First thorn bolt — Josie's work — sank three inches into the shoulder joint. Amber sap erupted. The proto-guard staggered. Its left arm dropped, the reaching thorn-cluster going limp, and its advance slowed from deliberate to labored.

The south-wall spawn fired on schedule. Three Thornlings. The warriors rotated — two peeling off from the choke to face the new threat while two held the pinch. Marcus called the rotation at conversational volume, and the fighters moved as if they had been doing it for months instead of minutes.

Rex was everywhere and nowhere.

Not engaging. Never engaging. Working the perimeter the way he'd worked the south pasture during a rotational graze — a ghost at the edge of the herd, appearing wherever a path threatened a non-combatant, gone before anyone registered the intervention. One Thornling circled toward Heather's elevated position. Rex materialized on its flank, snapped at the root-leg, peeled, snapped again — two precise bumps that moved the creature two steps left into a **Warrior** whose blade was already swinging. Rex was six feet away before the Thornling hit the floor. Heather never saw the threat.

Judge held the south wall.

Forty pounds of Blue Heeler planted at the mouth of the south corridor with the finality of a dog who had decided the corridor was her fence line and intended to die on it before she let anything past. A Thornling came around the corridor's elbow at a low half-run. Judge launched off her hindquarters, found the trailing root-ankle, and set her weight backward — forty pounds anchored against the creature's forward momentum. The Thornling pitched. Recovered. Swung a thorn-arm at her and missed by a foot because she had already released and circled. A **Warrior** closed the gap. Judge barked once at the kill — sharp, possessive — and reset her stance at the corridor mouth.

The fence line held.

Second thorn bolt.

The proto-guard's right arm joint was next. **Structural Analysis** had mapped the seam while the creature was staggering from the first hit — the armor plates shifting on the left, new gaps opening on the right as structural integrity compensated for the damaged side. Nate sighted through the peep ring. Released. The bolt punched through bark and the right arm locked at the elbow, the thorn-cluster dropping to the creature's waist like a broken limb on a tree that had not quite fallen.

Third bolt.

The curved one.

Josie's vindicated thorn — the one Flint had called decorative at the Rest Area 1 bench and Josie had proved structural with the matter-of-fact calm of a woman who did not require Flint's approval to be right. The thorn had a natural rightward pull; Nate had filed a compensating flat into the base the night of its making, a micro-correction that let the curve fly true if the shooter knew how to read it.

Nate read it. Sighted for the shoulder-to-torso gap — the one where the creature's damaged arms had started to pull the chest plates apart, widening the seam **Structural Analysis** had been painting all fight. Compensated for the rightward pull. Released.

The bolt flew true.

Buried in the shoulder-to-torso gap. Amber sap sprayed in a thick arc, the kind of arc a vet pulled from a steer whose heart had given up. The proto-guard seized. Both arms locked. Its advance stopped completely, seven feet of bark-armored proto-monster standing in the center of the room — rooted, locked, a dead tree still vertical.

The warriors finished it.

Two men converging on the immobilized creature, hacking into the joints Nate had already cracked open. Not surgical. Not pretty. But they attacked the same target from two angles without getting in each other's way, and three rooms ago that would not have been possible. Eight seconds of ugly, coordinated violence, and the proto-guard collapsed in a sound of splitting bark and tearing root-fiber that echoed off the stone walls.

Amber sap pooled beneath the carcass.

---

The room exhaled.

For two seconds — maybe three — there was no sound but heavy breathing and the soft *tock-tock* of amber beading off the proto-guard onto the stone. Heather was hunched on her ledge, hands at her knees, mana faintly visible at her fingertips like coals banked under ash. The smell was saturating now — scorched sap, iron, dust, the green-wood reek of split bark concentrated into the low fifty feet of breathing room they had. Two warriors were laughing, short and ugly, the sound of adrenaline with no exit.

But the room had done what the room was designed to do.

The rock formations that made positioning possible also made isolation possible. A **Warrior** — young, linebacker-sized, one of the stronger fighters in the group — had pushed too far east chasing a Thornling into a corridor between two stone ridges. Good instinct. Bad geometry. The corridor narrowed. His sword arc shortened. Two Thornlings came around the far end and caught him from a direction his blade could not reach. He killed one. The second killed him. By the time another fighter reached the corridor, the young man was on the stone and the Thornling had already moved on.

Behind the west rock formation, two non-combatants had tried to shelter.

A man and a woman. Both had read the elevated terrain and cover positions as safety instead of what they actually were — kill boxes with one exit each. A Thornling from the south-wall spawn found the gap between the rocks. The man had been carrying a shield he barely knew how to hold. He put it between the creature and the woman behind him, and for two seconds the shield held.

Then the thorn-cluster punched through the wood and through the arm holding it, and the man went down.

The woman tried to run.

The corridor was six feet long with one exit. The Thornling was between her and it.

Nate heard the sounds. He could not see them — the rock formation blocked line of sight from his elevated position, and **Structural Analysis** did not read through three feet of quarried granite. By the time a **Warrior** cleared the corridor, there were two more bodies on the stone floor.

Three more dead. Forty.

The number had stopped being arithmetic.

Nobody said the names. That was the part that stayed with Nate — nobody said the names because almost nobody knew them. Forty-three had walked into the room and forty had walked out, and even the three who had not were becoming abstractions faster than felt decent. The young **Warrior** who had chased too deep. The man who had held his shield until it broke. The woman behind him. They would be remembered by what they had done at the end, not who they had been before it.

*The names were there. She kept them. That was part of the work. The young Warrior was twenty-four and had a sister in Lincoln, Nebraska, who would not know for a long time what he had become at the end — only that he had been loved by a family and was gone. The man with the shield was a middle-school shop teacher from Bartlesville who had fixed his neighbors' lawnmowers for free and never let the favor be mentioned. The woman behind him was his wife of thirty-two years — three grandchildren, a garden that would not be weeded this spring. The cohort did not know. The cohort did not need to know. But the names were kept, mind. The names were always kept.*

Sam Hargrove was standing near the east wall.

The same man who had glanced at Nate's **PROCESSING** tag in the starting room four rooms back and walked away with the calculation of rank and classification behind his eyes — *unclassified, unconventional, not worth the investment.* The look of a Guardian who had spent a career ranking assets and did not intend to spend this morning changing his rubric for a man in a work coat.

He was looking at Nate's elevated position now. At the crossbow with the iron peep sight. At three thorn bolts buried in a proto-guard's joints from forty feet.

He nodded. Once. Short.

The nod of a man whose professional assessment was being revised against his preference, and who had enough integrity to register it without enough flex to say so out loud.

Nate took that for what it was.

Something shifted in his peripheral.

Not the blue — something else. A warmth at the far edge of his HUD, lower-right, gone before he could turn to focus on it. Not amber. Not blue. Warmer than either. Gold, maybe, but so brief it could have been a trick of torchlight reflecting off the dissolving proto-guard's sap.

Rex's head snapped toward it.

Not toward a Thornling. Not toward a threat. Toward a point in the empty air six feet from where Nate stood, where there was nothing — no spawn point, no creature, no architecture **Structural Analysis** registered. Rex stared at the nothing with both ears forward and his tail held perfectly still, the posture of a dog who had seen something that existed outside the spectrum his person could perceive.

Then it was gone. Rex's ears relaxed. His tail swept once. He looked back at Nate with an expression that was not distress, not warning, but *recognition.* As if something he had been waiting for had looked back at him, and he was satisfied.

"Flint. Did you catch that?"

Silence. The kind of silence Flint filled when he did not want to answer a question directly. The halting, uncharacteristic pause of a voice that had something to say and was choosing what not to.

"Catch what?"

"Rex just—" Nate stopped. The gold was gone. Rex was back to scanning the room. The moment had passed like a deer crossing a trail — there and gone and impossible to prove.

"I registered a minor sensor anomaly," Flint said, after a pause that was definitely not a processing delay. "Probably interference from the proto-guard's dissolution. Higher-density material breakdown produces residual energy signatures that can—"

"You're making that up."

Another pause. "I'm *extrapolating* from limited data. Which is different from making it up in that it sounds more credible and I can cite sources."

"Cite one."

"I'd rather not."

Flint had seen something. And Flint had decided, in the space between detection and report, not to say what it was.

The things Flint didn't say were starting to form a pattern.

---

The last Thornling went down.

Not with ceremony — with the same root-fiber pop the others had made — and for a full five seconds the room held its breath waiting for the ninth spawn-point to cycle again. It did not. The ninth spawn was dark. The center of the room was stone and amber and the settling dust of eight bark-plate carcasses and one proto-guard.

Then the banner.

Blue panel, unmistakable, larger than any screen the **System** had yet put in front of the cohort. It opened in Nate's vision the way a good flag unfurls — snap at the top, settle at the corners, clean text centered across the field:

**FLOOR 1 — COMPLETE**
**Cohort Remaining: 40**
**Combat Rooms: 3 of 3**
**Specialization Pathways: Unlocked**

The pulse of it was subtle. A faint warmth behind the words, not quite heat, more like the way a good woodstove registered from across a room. Every member of the cohort had gone still over a screen of their own. Nate knew because Marcus had gone still. Heather had gone still. Walt Keane had stopped working his bark-plate fragment. The thirty-three others in the chamber were all doing the same thing at the same time, with the stillness of people whose screens had just gone polite.

A subtler chime sounded. Not the **Power Strike** pulse. Deeper. A low tone, three beats, the kind of chime a bank's vault door made when it decided to open.

At the center of the room — where the proto-guard had died, where the amber sap had pooled — the stone slid aside on a seam **Structural Analysis** had not registered thirty seconds ago. A stairwell opened. Not a corridor. Stairs, going down, lit by the same steady amber torchlight as the rest areas, fading into a depth the light did not reach.

**Floor 2 Access Available.**

"Oh," Flint said. "That's different."

Marcus was already at the stair-head, reading the geometry with the unbothered patience of a man who had walked into enough doors in his career to treat a new one as a logistical problem rather than a surprise. He glanced back at the cohort. At Heather on her ledge, banking mana. At Josie, who had picked up three bark-plate fragments from a fallen Thornling without breaking the flow of her conversation with the leather-worker. At Sam Hargrove, still standing near the east wall, still looking at Nate's position.

"Floor 1 is done," Marcus said. Flat. Fact-voice, not sermon-voice.

"Forty," Nate answered. Also flat.

"That's the one."

*Floor 1 is a shape the Tutorial uses to teach the cohort what cohorts look like. The thirty-six who descend the stairs are not the same people the forty-six who entered Room 1 were. Some changes are earned and some are grafted, and the hardest work of the floors that follow will be telling the difference. She does not say this aloud, love. She does not need to. Stairs down are a kindness, because stairs down mean the climb is already behind. Stairs down are also not a kindness, because stairs down are always down. Both things are true. Both things will stay true for a long time.*

Nate sat at the workbench one more minute.

Rex set against his boot. Judge against his other thigh. The crossbow across his knees — seven bolts remaining, two thorn-tipped, five reinforced rack, Josie's curved one spent in a proto-guard's shoulder and the memory of its flight still in his hands. The iron peep sight caught the torchlight. A small ring of metal that represented the difference between feel and measurement.

Rex's tail moved once. Forward.

Judge didn't wait for a tail sweep. She was already walking toward the stairwell.

*Handle your business.*

Nate stood. Scratched behind Rex's ears. Picked up the crossbow. And followed his dogs down.
