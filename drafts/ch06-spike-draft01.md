# Chapter 6 — Spike

---

The trees were wrong the same way the torches had been wrong — too even, too deliberate, the kind of forest that existed in screensavers and fantasy art and the manicured imaginations of people who'd never been lost in actual woods. Nate's boots hit loam instead of stone and the change registered like a slap. Not East Texas loam — nothing with clay content or the sullen red tint of Panola County dirt. Generated topsoil. Soft, uniform, the consistency of potting mix from a big-box store. His body wanted to believe the sky overhead was real, and his body was wrong.

**Structural Analysis** fired on the canopy before he'd taken three steps into the clearing.

Render boundary. Sixty feet up, where the treetops met blue, the simulation's ceiling pretended to be atmosphere. The seam was subtle — most people wouldn't notice, the same way most people didn't notice where a good paint job met the trim. But **SA** read structural boundaries like property lines on a survey, and the sky stopped being sky and started being infrastructure exactly where the **System**'s environment engine had decided that "up" wasn't worth rendering anymore.

Different tileset. The **System** rotated encounter environments — forest clearing after three rooms of stone corridors. Diablo did this. Act 1 forest, Act 2 desert. Same enemies, new wallpaper. Except wallpaper didn't have spawn points, and this clearing had sixteen of them pulsing under the generated loam like infected heartbeats.

New pasture. Same cattle.

"Well," Flint said. "The Tutorial has a landscaping budget. I'm genuinely impressed. The foliage rendering is — wait." His voice dropped from theatrical to terse in the space between syllables. "Sixteen spawn points. Two of them are running a different density profile than the others. Heavier. Slower cycle. Whatever comes out of those two is not the same thing that comes out of the others."

"How different?"

"Significantly. Think the difference between a calf and a bull, since we're apparently committed to the cattle metaphor for the rest of our lives."

Rex was already low, shoulder pressed against Nate's shin, eyes sweeping the clearing with the focused precision of a dog who had entered unfamiliar terrain and was cataloging every movement, every shadow, every place where something could hide and the places where something was already hiding and hadn't moved yet. His body carried an impression that washed through the bond — not words, never words, but the whole-body tension of an animal who had spent four years reading danger in pastures and had just read this clearing as something worse.

Judge flanked wide. Where Rex went still and low, the Blue Heeler went forward — forty pounds of blue-speckled aggression already circling to the clearing's east edge, nose working, body coiled into the driving crouch that meant she'd identified a threat and was deciding which direction to push it. She didn't wait for Nate's read. She never waited. Rex gathered information; Judge gathered momentum.

*Something bigger. Something that doesn't move like the others.*

Rex had felt them two seconds before Flint's sensors registered the density shift. Judge had felt them one second after Rex and was already angling toward the heavier signatures, because Judge's response to *something bigger* had always been *then I'll hit it harder.* The empathic sense beating the AI's environmental scan. Again.

Marcus was already calling formation from the corridor mouth, his **Vanguard** HUD painting positional data across the fighters' combat interfaces. "**Warriors**, spread formation — we've got room to maneuver. Don't bunch up. **Mage**, find elevation — there." He pointed to a cluster of boulders along the north edge, waist-high, good sight lines. "**Healer**, behind the rock line. Support, stay mobile."

Forty-nine people. All of them — every last body that had walked out of the safe room and fought through three rooms of Thornlings without losing a single soul. Three Healers. Josie. Nate. Rex. Judge. And the rest — the ones who weren't combat classes but had survived three rooms of learning that survival wasn't optional. Nobody had died yet. That fact sat in the group like a held breath, fragile and heavy at the same time.

The spawn points fired.

Eight Thornlings came through the loam in the familiar eruption of bark and dirt and wrong life — pulling themselves up, thorn-arms reaching, the blind seeking hunger that Nate had learned to read across three rooms of fighting. Routine. Three rooms of routine. Warriors engaged with downloaded precision, **System** techniques guiding blades into joints that combat HUDs highlighted in convenient orange. The **Mage** threw a controlled **Fire Bolt** — she'd learned her lesson about mana burn in Room 2 and now she waited, timed, spent what was needed and nothing more.

Then the other two spawn points opened, and the routine died.

The **Thornguards** didn't erupt. They rose.

Slow. Deliberate. Six feet of bark-plate armor pushing up through the forest floor like old-growth stumps deciding they'd had enough of being stepped over. The bark was different — thicker, layered, the kind of laminate plating that Josie had been collecting fragments of since Room 1 but had never seen intact on a living frame. Chest armor that covered shoulder to waist in overlapping plates. Arms that ended in forearm spikes instead of thorn clusters — longer, heavier, built for punching through things that the basic Thornlings' raking attacks couldn't scratch.

The first **Thornguard** straightened to its full height and the clearing went predator-quiet — every prey animal in range deciding simultaneously that stillness was better than speed.

*New room, new wallpaper, and the Interior Decorator from Hell just introduced armored variants. Pretty enough to fool you into thinking you weren't about to lose something. The **System** had the aesthetic sensibility of a dentist's waiting room — but darling, the drills were real.*

A **Warrior** — the man with the lion-embossed shield, the one who'd been reliable through three rooms of competent, System-assisted fighting — charged. His sword technique fired clean, the blade finding the gap between shoulder and torso that had worked on every Thornling since Room 1. The edge hit bark-plate.

And bounced.

Not glanced. Not deflected. *Bounced* — the blade meeting resistance it wasn't designed for, the thin-gauge steel that **SA** had read as cosmetic garbage in the first room ringing off armor that was everything the sword was not: dense, layered, honest material built to stop exactly the kind of attack the **System** had been training these fighters to deliver. The **Warrior** staggered back two steps, his sword arm vibrating from the impact, his face showing the particular shock of a man who'd just discovered that three rooms of success hadn't prepared him for the difficulty spike the Tutorial had been building toward.

F-rank weapons hitting their ceiling. The **System** scaled the weapons to handle basic Thornlings and then introduced an enemy that made those weapons irrelevant. Same thing that happened in procurement when the spec changed mid-contract and the hardware that passed last month's test couldn't pass this month's.

Judge hit the nearest Thornling from behind before the Warriors had fully engaged — forty pounds of Blue Heeler driving into its root-legs at a dead sprint, teeth finding the joint where bark met ground and *pulling*. The creature staggered. A **Warrior**'s blade found its chest while it was off-balance. Judge was already gone, wheeling toward the next one, driving it sideways into the kill zone that Rex's stare was herding it toward. Header and heeler. Rex positioned; Judge punished. The pair worked the Thornling pack like livestock that didn't know it was livestock yet — Rex holding the eye, applying pressure from the front, and Judge charging from behind to bite and drive anything that didn't move fast enough.

The **Mage**'s **Fire Bolt** hit the second **Thornguard** in the chest. Bark ignited — orange flare, heat bloom, the surface layer crackling and splitting. For half a second it looked like it worked. Then the fire died against the second layer and the **Thornguard** kept moving, trailing smoke, the chest plate cracked but not penetrated. The **Mage**'s face said everything her mana bar was already confirming: that bolt cost a quarter tank and bought three seconds.

"The chest plate is non-uniform," Flint said, combat-flat and fast. "Thicker on the front, thinner on the sides. Lower back is single-layer — the growth pattern concentrated armor forward. And the arm joints — watch the arms."

Nate was watching. **Structural Analysis** had painted the **Thornguard**'s arms as they swung — the forearm spikes extending forward on the attack, then resetting to guard position. Between extension and reset, the bark plates at the elbow separated. A gap. Half a second. The armor breathing open on the backswing before it sealed again.

The joint seams were decorative. **SA** laid out the architecture in clinical wireframe — the bark plates overlapping at the elbows and shoulders had visual continuity but no structural backing. The armor *looked* like it sealed at the joints. It didn't. The growth pattern had prioritized surface coverage over load-bearing connections, and the seams between plates were cosmetic filler over air. Pretty draftsmanship. No engineering.

"Lower back, single-layer. Arm joints — the seams are fake. No structural backing behind the plate edges." Nate called it from the boulder line, loud enough for the Warrior line to hear. "The armor's decorative at every joint."

The **Shield Guardian** was the first to move. The big man pivoted without hesitation — no longer the dismissive assessment of the first room, no longer the grudging nod of Room 3. Three rooms of watching Nate call every weakness from elevation had done what argument never could. He drove his shield into the **Thornguard**'s front, pinning it, turning it, and the two Warriors who'd learned to listen to the man with the barn-crossbow circled to the exposed back.

Their swords found single-layer bark. The blades sank.

The **Thornguard** screamed — deeper than the basic Thornlings, a sound like a ship timber cracking under load, and the ground vibrated with it. It went to one knee. The Warriors worked the gap, blades finding the exposed lower back, and the creature collapsed forward into the loam with a sound that was half falling timber and half something alive enough to make the victory taste wrong.

The second **Thornguard** was still moving.

Nate sighted through the peep ring. Forty feet. The **Thornguard**'s left arm was mid-swing — forearm spike driving toward a **Warrior** who'd overcommitted on a basic Thornling and hadn't seen the bigger threat closing from his flank. The arm joint was opening. The decorative seam splitting wide, the cosmetic bark filler parting like stage curtains to reveal the gap that **SA** had already filed.

He squeezed.

The thorn bolt crossed forty feet of dappled light and hit the elbow joint in the half-second window between extension and reset. Bark split. The bolt sank to the base and the **Thornguard**'s left arm dropped — not severed but compromised, the forearm spike swinging loose on damaged structure. The creature turned toward Nate's position with the slow deliberation of something that had identified the source of its damage and had opinions about it.

Rex materialized between the wounded **Thornguard** and Josie, who was already on one knee beside the first **Thornguard**'s corpse, hands working the bark-plate loose with the focused intensity of a woman who had found materials she needed and was not going to let something as inconvenient as an ongoing fight prevent her from collecting them. The dog held position — low, still, the stare locked on the advancing creature — and the **Thornguard**'s path bent two degrees east, away from the **Appraiser** and toward the Warrior line where three swords were waiting.

It went down hard. Took four Warriors and eight seconds of System-assisted violence, and when it was done the clearing smelled like burning wood and sap and the particular chemical wrongness of things that had been alive in a way that wasn't quite life and were now dead in a way that wasn't quite death.

Mini-boss encounter. The Tutorial just introduced elites. Everything before this was the warm-up round.

Silence. Heavy breathing. The **Mage** sitting on the boulder with her hands dark and her mana bar somewhere near empty. A **Warrior** looking at his sword — the blade bent fifteen degrees from the **Thornguard**'s chest plate, the thin-gauge steel deformed exactly as **SA** had predicted it would three rooms ago. Another **Warrior** with a gash across his ribs where a forearm spike had caught him on the half-turn, bark thorns leaving punctures that the **Healer** was already working on with dimmer hands than anyone wanted to see.

People who'd felt confident after Room 3 were shaking now. Three rooms of building competence, three rooms of learning the rhythm, three rooms of *nobody dies and the system works and we're getting better at this* — and one fight with two armored enemies had shattered all of it.

"Casualties?" Marcus said, and the word held the weight of a man who was asking for the first time and praying the answer was small.

"Zero fatalities." Flint ran the count through Nate's combat HUD — the simplified readout, green arcs and amber tag, nothing like the detailed stat pages the others had. "Four injuries, two moderate. One sword deformed beyond field use. And the **Mage** is at nine percent mana, which I mention because single digits make me philosophically uncomfortable. I've seen vending machines with more reserves."

Josie stood up from the first **Thornguard**'s corpse holding a piece of chest plate the size of a dinner plate. Three-layer laminate bark, intact, still warm from whatever passed for body heat in a creature grown from wood and malice.

"This is the best piece of armor material I've seen since the first room," she said. Her voice was steady but her hands were shaking, and the gap between the two told the real story. "The chain shirts are real metal but they're sized for adults. This" — she held the plate up — "can be shaped. Cut. Fitted. I need twenty minutes and a workbench and I can reinforce every exposed joint in the front line."

Nobody told her to stop. Three rooms ago someone would have. Three rooms ago the **Warrior** with the lion shield would have walked past her with the expression of a man who'd decided that the weird girl sorting monster parts wasn't his problem.

He was standing next to her now, holding his bent sword, waiting for her to finish.

---

Room 5 was a cave, and the cave was a kill box.

**Structural Analysis** read the architecture as they entered and Nate's stomach dropped before the first spawn point fired. The chamber was smaller than the forest clearing — forty feet across, irregular walls, water dripping from a ceiling that threw echoes in every direction. Sound bounced wrong in here. Footsteps multiplied. Voices came from everywhere and nowhere. The **System** had built an acoustic nightmare on purpose.

The spawn point placement was wrong. Not random wrong — deliberately wrong. Five spawn points clustered near the entrance, creating a wall of immediate combat that would pin the group at the choke point. Three more staggered along the sides. And two in the back — the same dense, slow-cycling signature that Flint had flagged in Room 4.

"It's a kill zone," Nate said. "The entrance spawn cluster is designed to stack the group at the door. While the front line is pinned, the side-spawns flank and the **Thornguard** in the back closes from range."

Marcus was already reading it. His **Vanguard** HUD painting the tactical picture in real time, processing the same data through a different lens. "We can't bunch at the entrance."

"No. We need to push through the first wave fast. Get to the center before the sides fire."

"That's aggressive."

"The room was designed to punish passive play."

Marcus looked at him for one second. Two. Then turned to the line. "**Warriors** — fast push. Don't hold the entrance. Drive through the first wave to center room. I want a defensive circle, backs together, before the second wave spawns. GO."

They went.

Ten Thornlings and a **Thornguard**. The numbers alone were worse than anything before — ten basic enemies plus an elite, in a space too small for the maneuvering that had saved them in the forest clearing. The Thornlings spawned at the entrance in a wall of bark and thorn and the Warriors hit them at a dead run, **System** techniques firing on contact, blades finding joints in the compressed space between the door and the first body. Not elegant. Not the careful, HUD-guided precision of Rooms 1 through 3. This was violence in a phone booth, shoulder to shoulder, the sound of steel on bark and bark on flesh and screaming that echoed off wet stone until the room was nothing but noise.

They broke through. Marcus's call — fast push, center room — saved them from the entrance kill zone, but the cost was disorder. Formation dissolved in the push. Warriors who'd been shoulder to shoulder were scattered, separated by Thornlings that had gotten between them, fighting individually instead of as a unit. The **Mage** couldn't find clear sight lines in the chaos. The **Healer** was already casting, mana flowing into a gash on a **Warrior**'s shoulder, her hands bright with the soft white glow that meant *someone was hurt bad enough to need immediate attention.*

The side-spawns fired.

Three more Thornlings, flanking from the east wall. Rex was already there — low, silent, the stare driving one toward the Warrior line before it had taken three steps. Judge came in behind the second — not herding, *attacking*, the Heeler's teeth finding root-leg and ripping sideways with a snarl that echoed off the cave walls and came back sounding like three dogs instead of one. The Thornling went down thrashing, bark-limbs scraping stone. But the third got through, and a support-class woman who'd been holding a torch for light took a bark-arm across the side that sent her spinning into the wall.

Tyler was fighting like he always fought — big axe, bigger swings, the System-assisted precision giving his seventeen-year-old frame the combat timing of a trained soldier. Axe Technique fired and the blade buried itself in a Thornling's chest. Clean kill. The technique greyed out. Cooldown.

The **Thornguard** came from the back of the room.

It moved through the cave with the deliberate, ground-eating stride of something that had been designed to arrive after the chaos started — after the formation broke, after the Healers were already spending mana, after the fighters were scattered and breathing hard and the acoustic nightmare of the room was turning every sound into every other sound.

Tyler was between the **Thornguard** and the support line. His axe technique was still in cooldown. Three seconds of being human in a room that required something more. He saw the **Thornguard** coming and did what seventeen-year-olds did when something six feet tall and armored in bark plate was closing distance — he swung the axe.

Without **System** assist, the swing was human. Powerful — the kid was strong, genuinely strong, the kind of raw physical strength that came from whatever life he'd had before the **System** took it away — but unguided. The edge hit the **Thornguard**'s forearm plate and glanced. The follow-through pulled Tyler off-balance, his weight committed to a swing that hadn't connected, and for one second his guard opened.

The **Thornguard**'s forearm spike drove through the gap.

Not a slash. Not a raking strike like the basic Thornlings. A punch — the entire mass of the **Thornguard**'s arm behind a spike of hardened bark that was designed to penetrate the kind of armor the Tutorial had been handing out. The spike hit Tyler's leather chest piece and went through it the way a fence post goes through wet clay — no resistance, no deflection, just the sound of something that should have stopped and didn't.

Tyler made a sound. Not a scream. A sound that was smaller than a scream and worse — the compressed exhale of a body that had just been punctured and didn't have the air left to protest.

He went down.

The room kept fighting around him. That was the thing — the thing that would haunt the people who were there for longer than the death itself. The room didn't stop. The Thornlings didn't pause. The **System** didn't freeze the encounter for a moment of silence. Tyler hit the cave floor and the fight continued over him, and for seventeen seconds that felt like seventeen hours, a seventeen-year-old boy lay on generated stone with bark thorns in his chest while the room roared.

A **Healer** reached him. The woman — young, hands already dimmer than they'd been at the start of the fight, four rooms of constant triage wearing her mana down — calving-season exhaustion, the kind that hollowed you out from the inside — dropped to her knees beside Tyler and pressed her palms to the wound. The glow intensified. Flesh knitted at the edges. She closed the external damage the way she'd closed a dozen gashes and punctures across five rooms of combat. Skill and **System** working together, the healing protocol executing through her hands with the same efficiency that guided the Warriors' blades.

The wound closed.

Tyler didn't get up.

The **Healer**'s hands pressed harder. The glow brightened, then flickered, then steadied at a level that **SA** read as maximum output — everything she had, every point of mana pushing through her palms into a body that was taking the energy and not responding. She could close the wound. She couldn't undo what the spike had done inside. Internal damage — organs, vessels, the architecture of a body that leather armor was never designed to protect against a weapon that was designed to defeat it.

The fight ended. The last Thornling fell. The **Thornguard** went down under five Warriors who converged with the furious, grief-driven violence of people who'd just watched a kid take a hit that nobody should have taken. The cave went quiet except for the dripping water and the **Healer**'s breathing and the soft, fading glow of hands that were trying to do something they couldn't.

*This is where the story changes, love. Not the rooms. Not the monsters. Not the numbers climbing or the skills unlocking or any of the shiny mechanical things that make a man feel like he's getting stronger. Here. On this floor. With this boy. This is where it stops being a game for everyone in this room, including the one telling you about it.*

Tyler died with his eyes open and his interface still glowing.

Then the glow died too.

The blue panels that had been projecting across his vision — the character sheet, the health bar, the skill list, the entire System architecture that had been painted across a living brain — blinked once and went dark. Not a fade. Not a transition. A severance. The connection cut the instant the brain stopped interpreting the signal, and the screens that needed a living mind to project onto found an empty house and locked the door behind them.

Death killed the interface. The **System** needed a living host and Tyler wasn't one anymore.

The body didn't dissolve.

That was the second thing. Not the first — the first was the sound he'd made, the small sound that was worse than a scream. The second was the body. The Thornlings dissolved. The Tutorial's cleanup protocol broke them down within minutes, reclaiming the generated material, erasing the evidence. Human material didn't dissolve. The **System** didn't reclaim its participants. Tyler lay on the stone floor of a cave in a Tutorial pocket dimension, and the stone didn't take him back, and the body didn't fade, and everyone in the room had to look at the cost.

Rex pressed against Nate's leg. Not herding. Not working. Just — there. The stillness of a dog who had watched things die on the ranch and had learned that some deaths required presence instead of action. His body was warm and his breathing was steady and he didn't move.

Judge couldn't be still.

She paced a tight circuit around the body — not sniffing, not investigating, just *moving*. Three steps left, pivot, three steps right, pivot. The restless patrol of a working dog who had identified a problem she couldn't fix and whose entire operating system was built on fixing problems by driving toward them. Something was wrong. She could feel it — steer down in the pasture, the wrongness of a living thing that had stopped being living — and her response was the only response she had: keep moving, keep circling, keep working the perimeter of the thing that broke.

It wasn't going to make sense again. Judge kept pacing anyway.

Permadeath. No respawn. No save point, no resurrection mechanic, no "release spirit and run back to your corpse." The Tutorial had permanent consequences. Every room from this point forward carried a price tag denominated in something that couldn't be ground back.

**Structural Analysis** read the room's architecture — the spawn point placement, the entrance clustering, the delayed **Thornguard** deployment. The kill zone wasn't an accident. The **System** had built this death into the encounter the way an engineer built tolerances into a design. Calculated. Deliberate. The room's geometry was optimized to produce exactly what it had produced — a situation where someone who was strong but not fast enough, brave but not experienced enough, *young* but not experienced enough in the ways that kept you alive.

You bury your dead and keep working. That was the law of it. The law of calving season when you lost one, the law of drought years when the count came up short, the law of every ranching family that had ever stood over something that should have lived and didn't and then turned around and fed the ones that did. You bury your dead. You keep working. The grief has a place and the place is later.

Damn it.

"His name was Tyler," Flint said. His voice came through the shared space behind Nate's eyes, stripped of every layer — no theater, no deflection, no speed-as-armor. Just information, delivered with the weight of something that mattered not for tactical value but because the alternative was letting a name disappear into a number. "Seventeen years old. His System profile registered during the first room. Class: **Warrior** — basic melee. His combat efficiency was above the group median. He was improving faster than seventy percent of the fighters in this group."

A pause. The kind of pause that Flint filled with data when data was available and filled with nothing when it wasn't.

"He died because his cooldown timer lasted three seconds longer than the encounter allowed. The **System** built a room where that gap would be exploited. Not might be. Would be."

Nobody else could hear Flint. Nobody needed to. The AI told Nate because Nate was the only one who could listen, and because Flint was learning that some information mattered not because it was tactically useful but because the alternative was a silence that felt like complicity.

Marcus stood over the body with the expression of a man who had led people for twenty years and had just been reminded what that cost. His jaw worked. His hands opened and closed at his sides — not fists, not relaxed, the hands of a sergeant who'd written letters home and was about to add to the count. The **Vanguard** HUD was feeding him data — health bars, mana levels, the after-action metrics of a fight that had gone wrong exactly as the **System** had intended — and none of it mattered.

"We move him to the side," Marcus said. His voice was flat. Not cold — flat. Storm-pressed. Everything leveled. "We clear the room. We keep moving."

Nobody argued. Nobody had the energy to argue with the only correct decision available to a group of people who couldn't go back and couldn't stay and could only go forward through rooms that were going to keep doing this.

They moved Tyler's body to the east wall. Two Warriors carried him — one at the shoulders, one at the feet, the careful choreography of men who had never carried a body before and were trying to do it with the dignity that the situation demanded and the room didn't. They set him down gently. Someone — a woman whose name Nate didn't know — laid her jacket over his face.

Josie was collecting.

She moved to the **Thornguard**'s remains — fast, certain, same as always, hands finding material with the instinct that had identified real leather in the first room and structural thorns in Room 1 and every hidden piece of value that the group had walked past since the Tutorial started. Her hands were shaking. Her face was the face of a woman who had seen a boy die and was processing that information in the only way she knew how, which was to keep working, keep moving, keep her hands busy with materials that made sense in a world that had just stopped making any.

She worked around the body. Not over it. Around. A deliberate, careful geometry that cost her time and efficiency and was non-negotiable, the spatial respect of a woman who had run estate sales in homes where people had died and had learned that you worked the room but you didn't work the person.

"Someone has to," she said quietly. To nobody. To the room. To the part of herself that hated her hands for being steady when they should have been still.

She collected anyway. Because the living needed armor more than the dead needed everyone to stop and mourn on a timeline that the Tutorial wasn't going to honor.

"Three intact chest plates," she said, laying the pieces out on a flat stone with none of her usual theatrical commentary. "Two forearm sections. Enough to reinforce maybe four sets of armor." She paused. Swallowed. "Tyler's leather failed at the point of impact. If I'd had these plates an hour ago —"

She stopped. The sentence didn't need finishing.

---

The rest corridor between Rooms 5 and 6 was longer than the previous ones, and Nate understood why.

The **System** was giving them time. Standard game design — a rest zone before the next difficulty spike. Every MMO built decompression into the dungeon loop. Stressed players made bad decisions, and the Tutorial needed them functional for whatever came next.

Rest pen between chutes. Even cattle got that much.

The corridor widened into a crafting area larger than anything they'd seen. Workbenches along both walls. A tool rack that **Structural Analysis** read as comprehensive — files, hammers, tongs, clamps, things with handles and purposes. And against the far wall, something new.

A forge.

Small. Functional. Stone base, iron belly, bellows mounted on the left side with a leather-and-wood mechanism that **SA** read as genuine — real materials, real engineering, built to move air over coals. An anvil beside it, waist-height, the flat face worn smooth by use that hadn't happened yet because the **System** generated its infrastructure pre-aged, the same way a restaurant distressed its furniture to look established.

But the iron was real. The coals were real. The heat, when Nate worked the bellows and the coals glowed from dormant to active, was the honest heat of combustion — not the steady, generated light of Tutorial torches but actual fire doing actual work. His hands found the bellows handle and something settled in his chest the way it always settled when a tool was right and the work was clear.

**Field Repair** sang.

Not literally — there was no sound. But the skill's connection to materials intensified near the forge like a radio signal strengthening near the tower. His palms read the anvil's iron: dense, old, the crystalline structure of metal that had been heated and cooled and heated again until it forgot what it was like to be ore. The hammer in his belt loop hummed against his hip. The **Thornguard** plates Josie had laid on the workbench resolved into legibility through **SA** — grain direction, density maps, the natural stress lines that told him where the material wanted to bend and where it wanted to hold.

He pulled the crossbow onto the workbench. Three rooms of combat had tested every joint and connection. The prod's spring steel was still true — **SA** confirmed the temper held, the crystalline memory intact, the steel doing what good steel did. The stock had taken impacts. Micro-fractures along the grain where recoil had stressed the wood past its design intent, because the design intent had been "hold a prod" and the actual use had been "fire thorn bolts through armored bark at forty feet, repeatedly."

He cut a section of **Thornguard** chest plate to shape — the hammer's cross peen scoring the bark along grain lines that **SA** mapped, then a clean break along the score. The piece fit the stock's underside like it had been measured for it, because it had — **Field Repair** reading both materials simultaneously and finding the geometry where bark plate would reinforce wood without adding weight where weight would change the balance.

The bark bonded to the stock under **Field Repair**'s contact. Not glue. Not fasteners. The molecular persuasion that Nate still didn't fully understand and Flint was still trying to quantify — materials holding each other because they'd been properly introduced by hands that spoke their language.

The hammer's face got the same treatment. A cap of **Thornguard** plate over the flat striking surface, trimmed to preserve the balance that twenty years of tool use had made instinctive. The cross peen stayed bare — shaping work needed metal on metal, and bark plate would dampen the precision. But the striking face, the face that would hit things that hit back, gained three layers of bark laminate that **SA** read as significantly harder than the original steel.

Upgraded crossbow. Upgraded hammer. Twenty minutes of forge work that felt like five, because time moved differently when your hands were building and your brain was processing and the two spoke a language that didn't require conversation.

*Watch his hands. Not the crossbow, not the hammer — his hands. That's where you see it, if you know what to look for. The way a man who can't read his own character sheet reads everything else. Steel. Bark. The grain of wood under his palms. He doesn't know what he is yet. But his hands do.*

"TUTORIAL ADVANCEMENT REWARD — DISTRIBUTE AMONG YOUR PARTY."

The **System**'s text appeared in the blue that Nate couldn't access — but everyone else could. A crafting station along the east wall activated, surfaces glowing with the soft amber of System-generated equipment. Gear materialized in organized rows: reinforced leather armor, improved weapons, a shield with actual metal facing instead of composite pretending. Twenty-four sets. Laid out with the careful presentation of merchandise designed to be chosen.

Twenty-four sets.

Marcus counted heads. His **Vanguard** HUD doing the math that his face was already doing manually — the count, the gap, the equation that every raid leader recognized.

Josie Pickett, who had an opinion about everything and a comment for every occasion and a running monologue that could fill any silence within a three-room radius, said nothing. First crack. Small. The reader saw it. Josie didn't. Not yet.

He filed it.

The **Mage**'s boulder and its structural stress from the **Thornguard**'s approach angle.

Too much.

The wireframes dimmed.

Not vanished. Not deactivated. Dimmed — the overlay data dropping from full brightness to something like a whisper, still present at the edges of his vision but no longer screaming for attention. What the hell.

The wireframes brightened.

Full clarity. Six simultaneous overlays, each one distinct, each one legible — the same data that had overwhelmed him thirty seconds ago, restored to full resolution by an act of attention he hadn't known he could perform.

Too much. He dimmed them again. Selectively this time — pulling back the pillar data and the floor map, keeping the **Thornguard** armor profiles and the Thornling joint architecture, the combat-relevant layers that his targeting needed and nothing else.

The corridor beyond Room 6 forked, and the fork wasn't a choice.

But the dog was facing it. And the man was beside the dog. And behind them, seventeen people who'd been strangers six rooms ago were checking weapons and tightening armor and getting ready to walk into the dark because the only other option was standing still, and standing still wasn't something any of them knew how to do anymore.

Nate raised the crossbow. The upgraded stock felt solid against his shoulder — **Thornguard** plate over hardwood over spring steel, ugly and honest and dangerous. Three thorn bolts remaining. A forging hammer on his belt. An inventory ring on his finger. A voice in his head that had just named a math thing after a woman with a thorn collection, and had stopped being funny about it, which meant it was serious, which meant the numbers were bad.

And somewhere behind his eyes, a toggle he hadn't known existed. A dial that turned the world from noise to signal and back again. A tool he'd built by accident in the middle of a fight, using a brain that the **System** couldn't read and an interface that the **System** hadn't built.

"Let's go," he said.

Rex's tail swept once. Forward.
