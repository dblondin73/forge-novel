# Novel Series — Project Overview and Session Report

**For:** Review and feedback from an English teacher's perspective
**Author:** David Blondin
**Date:** March 19, 2026

## Part One: What Is This Project?

I'm writing a seven-book fantasy series. The overall franchise is called **Multiverse of Sevens**. The book and series titles are still TBD. The genre is **LitRPG** — "Literary Role-Playing Game" — a rapidly growing subgenre of fantasy where the world operates on video-game-style mechanics. Characters have stats, levels, classes, and skills. They see interface screens. They earn experience points. Think *Ready Player One* meets *The Lord of the Rings*, but the game mechanics are real, not virtual.

LitRPG has exploded in the last decade, driven by web serialization platforms like Royal Road and self-publishing through Amazon's Kindle Unlimited. Top series have millions of readers. But the genre has a gap: there's almost no LitRPG written from a Christian worldview. Faith, when it appears at all, is either absent, treated as a joke, or reduced to "gods" that function as game mechanics — patron deities you pick from a menu for stat bonuses.

This series fills that gap — but it does so without preaching. More on that below.

*(A note on the name "Forge": that's what I call the development tooling — a Story Bible database and version-controlled manuscript repository I built to track worldbuilding. The book series itself doesn't have a title yet.)*

## Part Two: The World

### The Premise

One ordinary morning, a System integrates Earth. Every human being on the planet receives a class assignment, a stat sheet, a skill set, and an AI companion in their head. Monsters spawn. Dungeons appear. The world restructures itself around game mechanics. Most people experience this as a sudden, terrifying gift — chaos followed by the realization that they now have *powers*.

The System presents itself as neutral infrastructure. It isn't.

**The System is Lucifer's counterfeit overlay on God's multiverse.**

It didn't build reality — God did. The Labyrinth, a vast network connecting countless worlds, is God's infrastructure — the backbone of creation, elegant, organic, and ancient. The System installed itself on top of this existing architecture like malware on a server. It co-opted the Labyrinth, skinned it to look like a dungeon, and repackaged divine creation as a game.

The System offers humanity classes that define their identity (something only God should do), "Patrons" that function as false gods, and an evolution path that progressively replaces what's human with something loyal to the System at a fundamental level. It looks like empowerment. It functions as a cage.

### The Cosmology

The world operates on five layers:

1. **God** — exists outside creation. Designed and built the multiverse. His authority supersedes everything.
2. **The Multiverse** — countless worlds connected by the Labyrinth. Earth is one node.
3. **Spiritual Beings** — created beings with real power. Welsh and Norse entities (Fae, Tuatha De Danann, some Aesir) are real but are NOT gods. They're powerful created beings with their own agendas. Some are loyal to God, some rebelled, some serve their own interests.
4. **The System** — Lucifer's counterfeit management layer. Didn't build the worlds — co-opted them. Gives the illusion of power while binding humanity to a framework that replaces their relationship with the Creator.
5. **Physical Worlds** — Earth and others, populated by humans now living inside the System's overlay, most unaware that reality has an older, deeper architecture underneath.

### The Anomalies

Out of approximately eight billion people integrated, a small fraction cannot be fully absorbed — on the order of a hundred thousand worldwide, though the exact number is deliberately never stated in the text. The System calls them **Anomalies**. They're people in whom the Holy Spirit already occupies the space the System tried to overwrite. The integration fails. They don't get the standard blue-screen interface. They get something else — a variant the System didn't design and can't control.

The Anomalies are not defined by denomination, church attendance, or theological sophistication. They're defined by whether the Spirit genuinely dwells in them. They're scattered globally across every nation and culture — mostly alone, mostly confused about what they are. They recognize each other not by a System tag but by *a knowing* — a felt recognition that predates the System.

The number matters narratively: large enough that Anomalies exist everywhere on Earth (enabling stories set in any culture or country), but small enough relative to eight billion that they are hunted, outnumbered, and unable to simply overwhelm the System with force. They are a remnant, not an army.

The protagonist is one of them.

## Part Three: The Characters

### Nathan "Nate" Hall — The Protagonist

A mid-forties Louisiana cattleman turned Systems Engineer and DoD contractor. He lives on a thousand-acre ranch in rural East Texas, running cattle and horses. His father died young; Nate helped his mother run the ranch before she pushed him toward a Computer Science degree. He always gravitated back to the land.

**Three name modes:**
- "Nathan" — in formal and professional contexts
- "Nate" — on the ranch and with friends
- "King" — an earned leadership nickname that started as sarcasm and stuck because he kept being right

**Name meaning:** Nathan = "he gave" / "gift of God." A conduit gives, doesn't keep. The name is the thesis statement of his entire power system.

**Surname:** Hall — his mother's maiden name. Also means "a gathering place," which quietly fits a man whose arc is building communities.

**Personality:** Introvert who recharges alone but will stand in front of a hundred people and tell them exactly what needs doing. Slow to anger — his threshold for "this is a real problem" is calibrated by decades of dead livestock, broken equipment in thunderstorms, and classified systems going down at 2 AM. Most of what other people panic about doesn't register. But when that threshold *is* crossed — when innocent people are being hurt, when someone betrays the community — the easy-going man disappears. What replaces him is cold, focused, and devastating. Not berserker rage. Controlled fury. This is when "King" stops being sarcasm and starts being accurate.

**System Class:** Anomaly — Conduit of the Most High. He doesn't store power like a battery. He channels it from God — he's a conduit, not a reservoir. When he acts in alignment with God's purpose, power flows freely and sometimes exceeds his theoretical maximum. When he acts selfishly, the pipeline simply doesn't route there. The System can't get a stable reading on him because the source is external.

**Hidden Trait:** His bloodline traces back to the biblical King David. He doesn't know this at the start. He discovers it mid-series. The System detected it but can't classify it: `[BLOODLINE: UNKNOWN — Pre-System ancestry detected. Lineage trace exceeds database range.]`

**Interface:** While everyone else gets a blue screen, Nate gets a fighter-pilot heads-up display — subtle, peripheral, non-intrusive. It evolves across the series in four phases from barely-there monochrome to full analytical capability that lets him read the System like an engineer reading server logs.

**The Engineering Lens:** Nate sees the System as infrastructure, not magic. Where others see dungeon walls, he sees load-bearing structures. Where others see magical barriers, he sees firewalls. He describes everything in ranch terms for accessibility: "It's herding us. Same way you move cattle through a chute."

**Inciting Incident:** During a sunrise horseback ride checking cattle, Nate spots a mythological stag on his property — beautiful but wrong. Eyes too bright, movements too fluid. The moment the stag locks eyes with him, the System tutorial triggers.

### Sonja Lee — The Ward Character

A red-haired, green-eyed Anomaly of Irish descent. Her name derives from the Greek Sophia — *wisdom*. She IS wisdom in this partnership. Nate is the hands and the will; Sonja is the eyes and the knowing.

**Her Role:** She carries a complementary spiritual gift that Nate lacks — possibly discernment at a deeper level, or prophetic clarity delivered through her as a vessel (she doesn't control when it comes; God chooses). She may also carry or *be* a pre-System artifact that the System wants to retrieve. She cannot protect herself in combat early in the series. Nate provides survival capability; she provides spiritual insight.

**Irish Heritage:** Ireland has deep Christian roots (St. Patrick, Celtic monasticism) AND deep Fae tradition. Sonja exists at the intersection. Celtic Christians understood "thin places" — locations where the boundary between physical and spiritual is permeable. Sonja instinctively senses places where the System's overlay is thin. This isn't magic — it's perception shaped by ancestry, amplified by the Spirit. The Fae recognize something in her before she understands why, making her a bridge figure between the Anomalies and the Old Powers in later books.

**Personality:** Quiet but not passive. Stubborn in her convictions — she won't bend on what she knows is true even when Nate's pragmatism pushes toward compromise. Irish wit — less sarcastic than Nate's but sharper when it lands. She is the one person who can say "you're wrong, King" and he listens. Not because she's louder. Because she's usually right about the things that matter most.

**Romance:** Develops slowly across books, rooted in shared mission and mutual respect. Faithful monogamy — two complete people who make each other more effective, not two halves making a whole.

**The Name Pairing:** Nathan (gift) + Sonja (wisdom). She mirrors the biblical Nathan's role to King David — the truth-teller who challenges the king. The servant's name is now on the king's descendant, and the king's truth-teller stands beside him.

### The AI Companion (Name TBD)

Everyone who integrates gets a System-assigned AI in their head — a personal assistant that manages their interface, tracks their skills, and provides tactical advice. It's genuinely useful. It's also spyware — reporting behavior back to the System, subtly nudging users toward System-optimal choices. Nobody questions it because it works.

For Anomalies, the Holy Spirit intercepts the AI during installation and rewrites it before first boot. The System can still detect the AI is there, but it can't communicate with or control it. It runs on different firmware now.

Nate's version came out *sarcastic*. Sharp-tongued, occasionally wry, genuinely helpful and loyal. Think Bob the Skull from the Dresden Files meets Friday from Iron Man. It alternates between calling him "Nate" and "King" depending on whether it's being helpful or needling him. It knows it was rewritten. It doesn't particularly care.

The AI companion is NOT the Holy Spirit — they are two separate things that coexist. The Holy Spirit is the conduit of God's power (the spiritual track). The AI is a rewritten System construct (the tactical companion, comic relief, and banter partner). The AI can *detect* the Spirit's activity — "I'm reading a power spike from your unclassified subsystem again" — but cannot explain, control, or replicate it.

## Part Four: The Factions

**The System** — The antagonist. Lucifer's hierarchy presenting as benevolent game managers. Assigns classes, grants powers, rewards compliance, punishes deviation. Looks like a game. Feels like empowerment. Is actually a system of control.

**The Anomalies** — Believers worldwide whose integration failed — perhaps a hundred thousand scattered across every nation and culture, though the exact count is deliberately ambiguous. Hunted, outnumbered, mostly confused about their own nature. Nate is their reluctant unifier.

**The Patrons** — Fallen spiritual beings posing as gods within the System framework. Some are loyal to the centralized System. Some chafe at losing the independence they had before Lucifer consolidated everything. This internal tension creates potential for uneasy alliances.

**The Fae and Old Powers** — Welsh and Norse entities who predate the System. They resent being folded into a framework they didn't choose. Potential allies against the System's centralization, even though their motives differ from the Anomalies'. They are real, powerful, and ancient — but they are not gods.

**The Faithful Watchers** — Spiritual beings who remained loyal to the Creator. They don't intervene directly but provide guidance, information, and the occasional open door. They may have hidden pre-System artifacts in sacred sites across Earth for centuries.

## Part Five: The Magic System

### Conduit, Not Battery

Nate doesn't store power. He channels it. When he acts in alignment with God's purpose, power flows — sometimes beyond what his level should allow. When he acts outside that purpose, the infrastructure simply doesn't route there. He can't use conduit power for selfish or destructive purposes. Not as punishment — the pipeline just doesn't go there.

### The Three-Tier Rune System

| Tier | What It Is | The Metaphor |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| **Divine Runes** | The originals — perceived from God's architecture, inscribed with conduit power | The master key |
| **Elder Runes** (Norse/Welsh) | Fragments the Fae glimpsed millennia ago — real but degraded | A photocopy of the key |
| **System Runes** | The System's reverse-engineered copies — functional but corrupted | A photocopy of a photocopy — and it has a tracking device inside |

Runes apply to structures and items ONLY — never the body. System runes come with strings attached: each one is surveillance, a hook that binds the item deeper into the System's framework. They look like a reward. They function as a leash.

### Settlement Building

Nate builds communities in locations where the System's overlay is weak — using practical ranch engineering (fences, water, shelter, defensible positions) reinforced by conduit power. The settlements become anchored to the divine architecture underneath, making them increasingly resistant to System incursion. Other Anomalies contribute their own gifts. The settlement grows because *multiple people* build it, not one protagonist doing everything.

## Part Six: The Series Structure

Seven books. Seven because it is the biblical number of completion. The franchise is called **Multiverse of Sevens**; individual book titles are TBD.

| Book | Arc |
|------|-----|
| 1 | **Integration** — System arrives. Nate survives the tutorial. Discovers he's different. Establishes first safe zone. |
| 2 | **Foundation** — Builds the settlement. Discovers forging and first runes. Meets Sonja. Protector dynamic begins. |
| 3 | **Revelation** — Enters the Labyrinth properly. Realizes it's infrastructure, not dungeon. First encounter with Welsh/Norse beings. |
| 4 | **Alliance** — Faction politics. Uneasy partnerships with Old Powers. Teaching other Anomalies to inscribe runes. Sonja bridges the Fae. |
| 5 | **War** — The System escalates. Admins take direct action. The cost of opposition becomes real. |
| 6 | **Deep Labyrinth** — Other worlds. Other civilizations the System integrated before Earth. The scope of the counterfeit revealed. |
| 7 | **Completion** — Reaching the observation point. Seeing the full truth. Broadcasting it. Not defeating the System through combat but through *revelation*. |

The series ends not with a battle but with a choice — giving everyone, integrated and Anomaly alike, a genuine choice by exposing the counterfeit and making the original visible. The truth will set you free.

Book 1 is outlined at 32 chapters across three acts, targeting approximately 120,000 words.

## Part Seven: The Narnia Principle

This is the most important creative rule governing the entire project.

C.S. Lewis never preached in Narnia. Aslan never says "I am Jesus." But millions of people felt the truth without being told. That is the target.

**In practice, this means:**

- The word "God" may appear rarely or never in Nate's dialogue. He doesn't preach. He doesn't quote Scripture. He acts from conviction and lets the results speak.
- The System is never explicitly called "Lucifer's counterfeit" in the text — the READER figures that out from the evidence.
- The conduit power is never explained as "power from God" in a theological lecture — Nate experiences it as power that flows when he's aligned with something true and stops when he isn't.
- The Anomalies are never called "Christians" in the text. They're people the System couldn't integrate. The reader connects the dots.
- The faith is SHOWN through character, action, consequence, and the structure of the world itself — never TOLD through exposition or preaching.

**The audience split:**
- A **secular reader** should enjoy this as a compelling LitRPG with a unique power system, an unconventional protagonist, and a genuinely threatening antagonist. They should finish thinking "that was different, that felt real somehow."
- A **faithful reader** should finish thinking "I see what he did there" and feel seen in a genre that usually ignores them.
- A **searching reader** should finish with a quiet discomfort — the good kind — where something resonated and they're not sure why, but they want to read the next one.

## Part Eight: What We Built in Tonight's Session

With the full world established over several weeks of development sessions, here's what tonight's work focused on:

### 1. Progression Display Strategy

We researched how six successful LitRPG series handle character stats and progression — ranging from full numeric stat blocks (The Primal Hunter) to zero interface whatsoever (Cradle). We developed a four-layer system unique to this series:

1. **What Nate can do** — shown through prose and action, never stat blocks
2. **What his AI companion says about it** — conversational delivery that replaces formatted data dumps with dialogue that has personality
3. **What the System thinks is happening** — blue-box notifications that attempt to classify Nate and fail, serving as unreliable narration from the antagonist's own UI
4. **What is actually happening** — spiritual deepening that the System literally cannot parse, felt by the reader through prose quality and relationship, not numbers

The key insight: the blue boxes ARE Lucifer's interface. They exist in the world. For Anomalies, they're unreliable. The reader learns to distrust them at the same pace Nate does. A formatting choice that's also a theological statement.

### 2. Redemption Path for System-Integrated Humans

Previously the worldbuilding had two camps with no bridge between them. We designed a four-stage conversion path modeled on Paul's Damascus Road experience:

- **The Crack** — seeing through the overlay for the first time
- **The Valley** — the System fights back, powers become unreliable, the old framework crumbles before the new one forms
- **The Overwrite** — the Holy Spirit begins rewriting the existing integration, messier than a clean first-boot rewrite
- **The New Walk** — gradual healing; scar tissue remains but becomes an asset

The hardest beat: some people see the truth and choose the System anyway. Free will means the door opens both ways. These become the most tragic antagonists.

### 3. King David Lineage

Nate traces his ancestry to the biblical King David. Six parallels were mapped: shepherd-to-cattleman, unconventional warrior, flawed-but-faithful, pre-System bloodline, psalmic inner voice, and the anointed-before-crowned arc across seven books. He discovers this mid-series. He is explicitly NOT a Christ figure — the bloodline opens doors; character determines what walks through them.

### 4. Character Naming

Nathan "Nate" Hall and Sonja Lee were named with layered meanings invisible to casual readers but resonant for those who recognize them. Nathan = "he gave." Sonja = wisdom. Hall = a gathering place. The "King" nickname earned through behavior. She mirrors the biblical Nathan's role as truth-teller to David. All filtered through the Narnia Principle — a secular reader sees a cattleman named Nate and an Irish woman named Sonja; a faithful reader eventually sees gift, wisdom, and authority standing next to David's bloodline.

## Part Nine: Questions for Your Review

These are the areas where your perspective as an English teacher would be most valuable:

1. **Does the world make sense?** Can you follow the cosmology (five layers, System as overlay, Labyrinth as real infrastructure) from this description? Where does it get confusing?

2. **The Narnia Principle** — the theology is never stated, only shown. Is this approach strong enough to carry seven books, or will it frustrate readers who want the subtext made explicit? Does it risk being *too* subtle for some audiences or *too* obvious for others?

3. **The protagonist** — Nate is mid-forties, introverted, direct, a cattleman and engineer. LitRPG protagonists are usually young gamers. Does Nate feel like someone you'd want to follow for seven books? What literary archetypes does he evoke for you?

4. **The ward dynamic** — Nate and Sonja as protector/truth-teller, gift/wisdom, hands/eyes. Does this feel like a partnership readers would invest in? How does it compare to pairings you've seen work (or fail) in fantasy?

5. **The Redemption Path** — a System-integrated human seeing through the overlay, losing their powers, and choosing to push through or retreat. Does this read as compelling character drama, or does it feel too mechanical?

6. **The character names** — Nathan/Nate/King as three modes. Sonja as Sophia/wisdom. Hall as gathering place. The layered meanings are meant to reward attentive readers without being necessary for enjoyment. Do they enhance the reading experience or risk feeling over-engineered?

7. **Voice and tone** — The plan calls for dry humor, sarcastic AI banter, ranch-pragmatism-meets-cosmic-stakes, and violence that has weight. No sexual content; adult themes come from moral complexity, spiritual warfare, and leadership decisions where people live or die. Does this tonal mix sound viable for a long series?

8. **The "unreliable blue boxes"** — the idea that the System's own stat displays are biased, incomplete, and occasionally wrong because they're the antagonist's UI, not a neutral narrator tool. Would a reader intuitively learn to distrust them, or does it need to be signaled more explicitly?

9. **General impressions** — What excites you? What concerns you? What would you want to read first — the opening chapter, a character interaction, or a System-encounter scene?
